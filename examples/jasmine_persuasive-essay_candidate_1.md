## PART A — Teacher Cheat Sheet

### Before the bell
- [[NEW]]Put Jasmine’s modified question page on her desk: the 4-part independent practice box plus the 4 MCQs with one-line “what this is really asking” notes. [[/NEW]] This keeps the lesson in the room with everyone else, but gives her a faster path into the task.
- [[NEW]]Lay a sticky note on the model essay with 3 labels: **thesis**, **evidence**, **counterargument**. [[/NEW]] She’s working toward the ELA goal: “**Accurately annotate a given text for the prompt — labeled annotation focus, supporting detail, etc.**”
- [[NEW]]Circle one spot at the front table for a 60-second check-in during partner reading and another during independent practice. [[/NEW]] Her profile says she does best with adult check-ins, but she prefers whole group, so keep her with the class and just touch base in place.

### Watch for
- [[NEW]]Head down after the model essay or after the first partner question. [[/NEW]] That fits her pattern: “**academic frustration → avoidance**.”
- [[NEW]]Repeated “Can I use the restroom?” right after the independent practice directions. [[/NEW]] That is one of her known shutdown moves when the task feels too open-ended.
- [[NEW]]Blank page / only writing a topic with no position or reasons during Independent Practice. [[/NEW]] Her present level says she “**often does not annotate**” and needs help “**to organize thinking**.”

### If they disengage
Use this exact 2-step script already named in the IEP:
1. [[NEW]]“Pause, breathe with me twice, then put your finger under the sentence that names the writer’s claim.”[[/NEW]]  
2. [[NEW]]“Now choose one calming strategy from your plan — deep breathing, movement break, fidget tool, sensory tool, or grounding — and then come back for the next line.”[[/NEW]]

Why this fits: Goal 1 — Counseling / Self-regulation says, “**Use a taught calming strategy (deep breathing, movement break, fidget tool, sensory tool, grounding) to calm and re-engage**.”

### Praise specifically
- [[NEW]]“You just labeled the thesis correctly — that shows you can find the writer’s main claim.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“Nice job using the evidence sentence to support your reason; that’s exactly what persuasive writing needs.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You stayed with the class and came back to the task after a break — that’s re-engaging like a strong writer.”[[/NEW]]

## PART B — Modified Lesson Plan

### 1) Whole-class opening discussion
- **Original step:** “Today we're going to start by looking at what makes an argument persuasive. Turn to your neighbor and talk about a time when someone convinced you to change your mind about something. What did they say? How did they convince you?”
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Keep her in the whole-group turn-and-talk, but hand her one sentence frame: “I changed my mind when ___ because ___.” If she can’t think of a personal example fast, let her use the model essay instead: “The writer convinced me by ___.”[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA: “**Independently answer literal and inferential comprehension questions or writing prompts that meet grade-level rubrics**”; present level: “**struggles with literal/inferential questions verbally and in writing**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]One index-card sentence stem at her seat. [[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]At the start of the lesson, before neighbor talk begins.[[/CHANGED]]

### 2) Introduce the model essay
- **Original step:** “Now, let's look at a model persuasive essay. Pay attention to: the clear statement of what the writer believes; the reasons and evidence they provide; how they handle disagreement.”
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Give her a three-color sticky-note guide and have her mark just three spots in the model essay: yellow for thesis, blue for evidence, pink for counterargument. Have her place the notes while you read aloud the first time, then point to each label during the second read.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA benchmark: “**Accurately annotate a given text for the prompt — labeled annotation focus, supporting detail, etc.**”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Three labeled sticky notes or a highlighter key. [[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]During the model essay read-aloud.[[/CHANGED]]

### 3) Partner reading analysis
- **Original step:** “Work with a partner to analyze the essay. Answer these questions together: 1. What is the writer trying to convince us of? 2. What evidence do they use? 3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?”
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Keep her with a partner in the room. Give her the questions one at a time on a strip. After each question, have her circle one answer choice from a mini-list of sentence starters instead of answering from scratch: “The writer wants us to believe…,” “One piece of evidence is…,” “This is convincing because….”[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA benchmark: “**Independently answer literal comprehension questions**” and “**Write a claim that accurately answers each part of the question**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Question strip + sentence-starter list. [[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]During partner reading, right after the model essay is read.[[/CHANGED]]

### 4) During-reading questions A–D
- **Original step:**  
  A. Think & Share — After reading the introduction, what do you think this essay will argue?  
  B. Turn & Talk — With your neighbor, discuss: Do you agree or disagree with the writer's main point so far?  
  C. Write — Jot down one piece of evidence the writer uses and whether it supports their claim.  
  D. Find Evidence — Highlight one sentence where the writer acknowledges a different point of view.
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Have Jasmine answer only A and C in writing on the modified sheet, and let B and D stay as partner oral responses with the class. For C, let her underline the evidence sentence in the essay first, then copy just 6–10 words into the box instead of a full sentence.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA benchmark: “**Independently find effective pieces of textual evidence to support the claim**” and “**Accurately annotate a given text for the prompt**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Modified response sheet with underline-then-copy box. [[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]During the reading questions, before independent practice.[[/CHANGED]]

### 5) Independent Practice: choose a social issue
- **Original step:** “Now it's your turn. Choose a social issue you care about—it could be about your school, your community, or something global. Write down: 1. The issue: 2. Your position (what do you believe): 3. Three reasons why you believe this: 4. Evidence for each reason.”
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Keep the same task and the same standard, but narrow the decision load: offer her a 3-choice topic bank that still fits the lesson’s social issue options, then have her complete the organizer with 1 issue, 1 clear position, 2 reasons, and 1 piece of evidence for each reason. If she finishes early, add the third reason.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA benchmark: “**Write a claim that accurately answers each part of the question**” and “**Independently find effective pieces of textual evidence to support the claim**.” Present level: “**Needs significant adult support to break down vocabulary, sentence structures, key ideas, and to organize thinking**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]3-choice topic bank + 2-reason organizer with optional third box. [[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]At the start of Independent Practice.[[/CHANGED]]

### 6) Small group discussion / peer feedback
- **Original step:** “In small groups, share your chosen issues. Help each other by asking: Is your position clear? Are your reasons strong? What evidence could support this better?”
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Keep her in the small-group share with the class. Give her one feedback card with two prompts only: “Your position is clear/not yet clear because ___” and “One stronger piece of evidence could be ___.” Let her read from the card if needed.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA benchmark: “**Write analysis showing how evidence supports the claim**”; strengths say she “**enjoys peer talk**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Feedback card with two stems. [[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]At the end of the lesson during group discussion.[[/CHANGED]]
