## Part B — Modified Lesson Plan

### 1) Whole Class Reading
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Today we're going to start by looking at what makes an argument persuasive. Turn to your neighbor and talk about a time when someone convinced you to change your mind about something. What did they say? How did they convince you?”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Before partner talk, hand Elena a 3-word choice strip: **school / family / internet** and ask her to pick one example so she is not searching for a topic from scratch. Then let her answer using one short sentence only: “I changed my mind when ___.”[[/CHANGED]]  
  [[NEW]]Why: Present Levels.Behavioral says she does better with “structured cooperative tasks with assigned roles,” and Goal 1 needs her to manage pragmatic load without shutdown.[[/NEW]]  
  **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]choice strip + one-sentence answer strip[[/NEW]]  
  **When:** [[NEW]]right before the neighbor talk at the start of Whole Class Reading[[/NEW]]

### 2) Model Essay Preview
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Now, let's look at a model persuasive essay. Pay attention to: - The clear statement of what the writer believes - The reasons and evidence they provide - How they handle disagreement”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Give Elena a pencil-in-the-margin cue box with three icons: claim, evidence, disagreement. As the class reads, she marks one example for each icon instead of taking general notes.[[/CHANGED]]  
  [[NEW]]Why: Goal 3 — ELA benchmark says “Generate one inference per paragraph during guided reading, with text evidence cited,” and the accommodation gives her the “Department-approved graphic organizer for inference/figurative items.”[[/NEW]]  
  **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]3-icon cue box[[/NEW]]  
  **When:** [[NEW]]during the model essay reading, before class discussion[[/NEW]]

### 3) Partner Reading
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Work with a partner to analyze the essay. Answer these questions together: 1. What is the writer trying to convince us of? 2. What evidence do they use? 3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Have Elena answer Questions 1–3 on a small response card with sentence starters: “The writer is trying to convince us that ___.” “One piece of evidence is ___.” “I think it is convincing because ___.”[[/CHANGED]]  
  [[NEW]]Why: Present Levels.ELA says her literal accuracy is strong, but “inferential … items are answered literally”; the sentence starters turn the hidden thinking into visible steps without changing the task.[[/NEW]]  
  **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]response card with sentence starters[[/NEW]]  
  **When:** [[NEW]]during Partner Reading, while the class works with a partner[[/NEW]]

### 4) During Reading Question A
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“After reading the introduction, what do you think this essay will argue?”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Ask Elena to circle one clue in the introduction and then write one prediction using the frame: “I think the essay will argue that ___ because ___.”[[/CHANGED]]  
  [[NEW]]Why: Goal 3 benchmark says “Apply the ‘clue → guess → check’ rule card to 5 idioms per week” and the present level shows she improves with “explicit ‘clue → guess → check’ steps.”[[/NEW]]  
  **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]circle-one-clue + prediction frame[[/NEW]]  
  **When:** [[NEW]]immediately after the introduction is read, before class share-out[[/NEW]]

### 5) During Reading Question B
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“With your neighbor, discuss: Do you agree or disagree with the writer's main point so far?”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Let Elena respond by pointing to one of two cards: **agree** or **disagree**, then add one reason from the text using the frame “I picked ___ because the text says ___.”[[/CHANGED]]  
  [[NEW]]Why: Present Levels.Behavioral says pragmatic breakdowns happen with turn-taking and topic switches; the forced choice lowers the language load while keeping the same text-based thinking.[[/NEW]]  
  **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]agree/disagree cards + reason frame[[/NEW]]  
  **When:** [[NEW]]during the Turn & Talk for Question B[[/NEW]]

### 6) During Reading Question C
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Jot down one piece of evidence the writer uses and whether it supports their claim.”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Give Elena a two-column box labeled **Evidence** and **Supports claim? yes/no**. She copies one sentence or short phrase, then checks yes or no.[[/CHANGED]]  
  [[NEW]]Why: Present Levels.ELA says “with visual scaffold accuracy rises to 75–85%,” so this keeps the reading demand high but makes the response concrete.[[/NEW]]  
  **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]two-column evidence box[[/NEW]]  
  **When:** [[NEW]]during the Write step in During Reading Questions[[/NEW]]

### 7) During Reading Question D
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Highlight one sentence where the writer acknowledges a different point of view.”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Pre-mark the paragraph numbers on her copy and ask her to highlight the sentence, then write “This is the counterargument because ___.”[[/CHANGED]]  
  [[NEW]]Why: Goal 3 benchmark asks her to “Distinguish literal vs figurative meaning…” and to use text evidence; labeling the move as counterargument keeps her from treating it as just “another fact.”[[/NEW]]  
  **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]pre-marked paragraph numbers + label frame[[/NEW]]  
  **When:** [[NEW]]during Find Evidence after the class reads the model essay[[/NEW]]

### 8) Independent Practice topic selection
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Choose a social issue you care about—it could be about your school, your community, or something global. Write down: 1. The issue 2. Your position 3. Three reasons why you believe this 4. Evidence for each reason”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Give Elena a 6-topic menu first: school lunches, homework load, recess time, hallway noise, recycling, phone use. She chooses one and completes the same 4 parts, but with one reason and one evidence source to start; then she adds the other two reasons after teacher check-in.[[/CHANGED]]  
  [[NEW]]Why: Present Levels.ELA says she writes best when the task is rule-based and concrete; the menu reduces topic-search time without lowering the persuasive writing demand.[[/NEW]]  
  **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]topic menu + staged planner[[/NEW]]  
  **When:** [[NEW]]the first 2 minutes of Independent Practice[[/NEW]]

### 9) Independent Practice drafting
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]Students write down the issue, position, reasons, and evidence.[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Have Elena fill in a persuasive planner with boxes in this order: issue → claim → reason 1 → evidence 1 → reason 2 → evidence 2 → reason 3 → evidence 3. She may type the final draft if handwriting slows her down.[[/CHANGED]]  
  [[NEW]]Why: Goal 3 requires grade-level writing access, and the response accommodation allows “Permission to type rather than handwrite when fine-motor fatigue triggers regulation issues.”[[/NEW]]  
  **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]persuasive planner + typing option[[/NEW]]  
  **When:** [[NEW]]the main Independent Practice work time[[/NEW]]

### 10) Student-Led Discussion
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“In small groups, share your chosen issues. Help each other by asking: Is your position clear? Are your reasons strong? What evidence could support this better?”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Assign Elena the role of “evidence checker.” She uses the 3 questions on the board and points to the matching part of her planner while she speaks. If she gets stuck, she may hand the group member her paper and point instead of explaining off the cuff.[[/CHANGED]]  
  [[NEW]]Why: Present Levels.Behavioral says she is strongest in “structured cooperative tasks with assigned roles,” and Goal 1 includes using a taught repair phrase when a breakdown starts.[[/NEW]]  
  **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]role card + board questions + pointer option[[/NEW]]  
  **When:** [[NEW]]during the 40–45 minute small group discussion[[/NEW]]

## Part C — Modified Student Materials

### A. Partner Reading Questions
**1. What is the writer trying to convince us of?**  
[[CHANGED]]A. A question about the topic  
B. A clear statement of the author's position or belief  
C. A list of all the evidence in the essay  
D. The conclusion of the essay[[/CHANGED]]  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: What does the writer want the reader to believe?[[/NEW]]  
**Answer: B**

**2. What is a counterargument?**  
[[CHANGED]]A. A reason that supports the main claim  
B. An opposing point of view that the author addresses  
C. The evidence used in the essay  
D. The introduction to the essay[[/CHANGED]]  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: What part of the essay shows the other side?[[/NEW]]  
**Answer: B**

**3. If you were writing a persuasive essay to your school principal about extending lunch time, which evidence would be MOST credible?**  
[[CHANGED]]A. “My friend thinks we need more time to eat”  
B. A study showing that students with longer lunch breaks have better afternoon attendance  
C. “Food tastes better when you eat it slowly”  
D. “I saw someone rushing through lunch yesterday”[[/CHANGED]]  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: Which choice is strongest because it is backed by a trustworthy source or data?[[/NEW]]  
**Answer: B**

**4. Which sentence is the best thesis statement for a persuasive essay?**  
[[CHANGED]]A. “Many students struggle with homework.”  
B. “Our school should require students to have one free period for homework instead of study hall.”  
C. “Some people like homework, and some people don't.”  
D. “Homework is a topic that people discuss.”[[/CHANGED]]  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: Which sentence clearly says what the writer believes and what should happen?[[/NEW]]

### B. During Reading Questions

**A. Think & Share**  
[[CHANGED]]After reading the introduction, what do you think this essay will argue?[[/CHANGED]]  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: What claim do you predict the author will make?[[/NEW]]  
**Sentence stem:** [[NEW]]I think the essay will argue that __________ because __________.[[/NEW]]  
**Checklist:** [[NEW]]
- I named the claim.
- I used one clue from the introduction.
- My answer is about the writer’s point, not just the topic.[[/NEW]]

**B. Turn & Talk**  
[[CHANGED]]With your neighbor, discuss: Do you agree or disagree with the writer's main point so far?[[/CHANGED]]  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: Do you think the writer’s claim makes sense so far?[[/NEW]]  
**Sentence stem:** [[NEW]]I agree/disagree because the text says __________.[[/NEW]]  
**Checklist:** [[NEW]]
- I picked one side.
- I used evidence from the text.
- I stayed on the topic.[[/NEW]]

**C. Write**  
[[CHANGED]]Jot down one piece of evidence the writer uses and whether it supports their claim.[[/CHANGED]]  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: Which detail does the writer use, and does it help prove the claim?[[/NEW]]  
**Sentence stem:** [[NEW]]One piece of evidence is __________. It supports the claim because __________.[[/NEW]]  
**Checklist:** [[NEW]]
- I copied or paraphrased one detail.
- I said yes/no about support.
- I explained why.[[/NEW]]

**D. Find Evidence**  
[[CHANGED]]Highlight one sentence where the writer acknowledges a different point of view.[[/CHANGED]]  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: Which sentence shows the other side or a counterargument?[[/NEW]]  
**Sentence stem:** [[NEW]]The counterargument is __________ because it shows __________.[[/NEW]]  
**Checklist:** [[NEW]]
- I highlighted one full sentence.
- I named the other point of view.
- I explained why it is the counterargument.[[/NEW]]
