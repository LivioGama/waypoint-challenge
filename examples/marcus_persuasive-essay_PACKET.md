# Tomorrow's Lesson — Marcus (Grade 7)

## Part A — Teacher Cheat Sheet

### Before the bell
- [[NEW]]Put headphones and the charged device at Marcus’s seat, already open to the lesson doc or slide with the model essay. This takes under a minute and matches **Assessment Accommodations**: “Text-to-speech for ELA reading passages and questions” and “Speech-to-text for open-response items.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]Print or open the modified student page with the MCQs and the short “what this question is really asking” lines. This keeps him in the same whole-group lesson while lowering the reading load, which fits **Present Levels (ELA)**: “Comprehension drops sharply on multi-clause sentences. With text-to-speech, comprehension ~grade level on the same text.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]Set a sticky note or small card on his desk with two self-advocacy words: “TTS” and “speech-to-text.” This is ready for a quick reminder if needed and lines up with **Goal 3 — Self-advocacy**: “Name his accommodations when asked” and “Set up TTS on a new device unprompted.”[[/NEW]]

### Watch for
- [[NEW]]He stops tracking the model essay when the teacher reads a sentence with several clauses, especially in **WHOLE CLASS READING** and **DURING READING QUESTIONS A–D**. That fits **Present Levels (ELA)**: “Comprehension drops sharply on multi-clause sentences.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]He starts joking, looking down, or going quiet when the class moves into **Independent Practice** and students have to choose a social issue on their own. That matches **Present Levels (Behavioral)**: “Becomes self-deprecating about reading in front of peers; will refuse to read aloud.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]He answers with a very broad issue (“school is bad”) instead of a specific persuasive topic when the lesson asks for a claim in **Independent Practice**. That usually means the prompt language is too open, not that he doesn’t understand the standard. This connects to **Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech**: “Dictate a complete claim sentence answering the prompt.”[[/NEW]]

### If they disengage
Use the IEP-backed two-step recovery script:
1. [[NEW]]“Marcus, use your TTS headphones for this next part so the reading comes to you.”[[/NEW]]
2. [[NEW]]“Then tell me your claim out loud or type it with speech-to-text — one sentence is enough to start.”[[/NEW]]

This uses his named accommodations from **Assessment Accommodations**: “Text-to-speech for ELA reading passages and questions” and “Speech-to-text for open-response items,” plus **Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech**: “Dictate a complete claim sentence answering the prompt.”

### Praise specifically
- [[NEW]]“You caught the writer’s position fast — that’s exactly what strong thesis readers do.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“That evidence works because you explained why it supports the claim, not just what the sentence said.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“Nice move asking for TTS and then getting right back to the task — that’s how a strong self-advocate works.”[[/NEW]]

## Part B — Modified Lesson Plan

### 1) Lesson launch: persuasive writing definition
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]Start with a conversation about times students have been persuaded.[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Keep Marcus in the whole-group turn-and-talk, but give him a 5-second preview card with two options: “school rule” or “community issue.” He chooses one example before he shares with a partner.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **Present Levels (Behavioral)**: “Responds well to choice (which scaffold to use) and to being treated as a capable thinker.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Choice card with two sample persuasion situations.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]At the very start of the lesson, during the opening conversation before the model essay.[[/CHANGED]]

### 2) Model essay introduction
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]Read the model persuasive essay and notice the claim, reasons, evidence, and disagreement.[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Give Marcus the text-to-speech version for the model essay and ask him to follow along with the print copy while the teacher reads aloud. Stop once after the first paragraph and ask him to point to the sentence that shows the writer’s claim.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **Assessment Accommodations**: “Text-to-speech for ELA reading passages and questions”; **Present Levels (ELA)**: “With text-to-speech, comprehension ~grade level on the same text.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Headphones + TTS copy + one highlight strip for the claim sentence.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]During the “Now, let’s look at a model persuasive essay” part of **WHOLE CLASS READING**.[[/CHANGED]]

### 3) Whole-class discussion after reading
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]Ask students to discuss what the writer is trying to convince us of, what evidence they use, and whether it is convincing.[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Before calling on the whole class, let Marcus rehearse one answer with his partner using the sentence frame, “The writer wants us to believe ___ because ___.” Then he can share orally; no cold call on reading aloud.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **Present Levels (Behavioral)**: “Will refuse to read aloud. Responds well to choice”; **Goal 3 — Self-advocacy**: “Politely decline unprompted oral reading without disengaging.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Sentence frame strip and partner rehearsal.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]During **PARTNER READING** questions 1–3 and the talk right after **DURING READING QUESTIONS A–D**.[[/CHANGED]]

### 4) During-reading questions A–D
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]Students answer Think & Share, Turn & Talk, Write, and Find Evidence prompts.[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Hand Marcus the shortened question page with a one-line gloss under each prompt. He answers A and B orally, writes only one evidence sentence for C with speech-to-text if needed, and highlights the counterargument sentence for D.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **Assessment Accommodations**: “Speech-to-text for open-response items”; **Present Levels (ELA)**: “Comprehension drops sharply on multi-clause sentences.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Modified question sheet with glosses, one highlight color for evidence, and speech-to-text for item C.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]Exactly at **DURING READING QUESTIONS A–D**.[[/CHANGED]]

### 5) Independent Practice topic selection
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]Students choose a social issue they care about and write the issue, position, three reasons, and evidence.[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Keep the same task, but narrow the starting move: he must first pick from three issue buckets written on a card — school, community, or global — then say one issue aloud before writing. After that, he fills in the organizer with speech-to-text for the claim and evidence boxes.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech**: “Dictate a complete claim sentence answering the prompt” and “Dictate two pieces of supporting evidence with paragraph references.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]3-bucket choice card + graphic organizer with claim/evidence boxes sized for dictation.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]At the start of **Independent Practice**.[[/CHANGED]]

### 6) Independent Practice: reasons and evidence
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]Students list three reasons and evidence for each reason.[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Let Marcus draft one strong reason first, then one evidence example, before adding the next reason. He does not need to finish all three reasons before the class moves on; he keeps working during the same lesson block with 1.5x time.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **Assessment Accommodations**: “Extended time (1.5x)” and **Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech**: “Edit dictated text for missing punctuation and homophone errors.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Reason-evidence organizer with one row highlighted as the “first row to finish.”[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]During the middle of **Independent Practice** while students are writing.[[/CHANGED]]

### 7) Small-group discussion
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]Students share issues and give feedback on position, reasons, and evidence.[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Place Marcus with his normal group. Give him one feedback stem on a card: “Your claim is clear because ___.” He may read his own claim from his paper or speak it; he does not have to read anyone else’s work aloud.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **Placement**: “Full Inclusion Program (services outside gen ed < 21% of time)” and **Present Levels (Behavioral)**: “Generally engaged and well-liked.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Feedback stem card and his own organizer in hand.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[CHANGED]]During **Student-Led Discussion** at the end of the lesson.[[/CHANGED]]
