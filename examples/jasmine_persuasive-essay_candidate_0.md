## PART B — Modified Lesson Plan

### 1) Lesson opener / prior knowledge
- **Original step:** “Today we're going to start by looking at what makes an argument persuasive. Turn to your neighbor and talk about a time when someone convinced you to change your mind about something.”
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Let Jasmine turn and talk with her preferred whole-group partner for 30 seconds, then call on her only if she volunteers; if not, she can point to one example from the class discussion notes and repeat the key idea in one sentence. [[NEW]]This keeps her in whole group, which she prefers, and lowers the writing load while still checking understanding. [[/NEW]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA: “Independently answer literal and inferential comprehension questions or writing prompts that meet grade-level rubrics, from 50% → 75%.” Present level: “Struggles with literal/inferential questions verbally and in writing.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Give her a one-line response strip: “Someone convinced me to change my mind when they _____.” [[/NEW]]

### 2) Model essay notice
- **Original step:** “Now, let's look at a model persuasive essay. Pay attention to: - The clear statement of what the writer believes - The reasons and evidence they provide - How they handle disagreement”
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Hand Jasmine the model essay with the thesis already lightly boxed and the counterargument paragraph number marked in the margin. Ask her to follow with her finger while you read and then circle the boxed claim after the read-aloud. [[NEW]]She needs the text broken into visible parts because she “often does not annotate” and “needs significant adult support to break down vocabulary, sentence structures, key ideas.” [[/NEW]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA benchmark: “Accurately annotate a given text for the prompt — labeled annotation focus, supporting detail, etc.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Boxed thesis, margin label: “counterargument,” and a 3-icon note strip: claim / evidence / disagreement. [[/NEW]]

### 3) Partner Reading
- **Original step:** “Work with a partner to analyze the essay. Answer these questions together: 1. What is the writer trying to convince us of? 2. What evidence do they use? 3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?”
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Have Jasmine answer only Question 1 first, orally with her partner, before moving to Questions 2 and 3. If she gets stuck, point her to the boxed claim and let her underline one matching sentence as evidence. [[NEW]]This keeps the challenge at grade level but reduces the amount she has to hold in working memory at once. [[/NEW]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA: “Independently answer literal and inferential comprehension questions…” and “Independently find effective pieces of textual evidence to support the claim.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Two-step partner card: “1. Claim, 2. Evidence.” [[/NEW]]

### 4) During Reading Questions A–D
- **Original step:** 
  - A. Think & Share: “After reading the introduction, what do you think this essay will argue?”
  - B. Turn & Talk: “Do you agree or disagree with the writer's main point so far?”
  - C. Write: “Jot down one piece of evidence the writer uses and whether it supports their claim.”
  - D. Find Evidence: “Highlight one sentence where the writer acknowledges a different point of view.”
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Ask Jasmine to answer A and C in writing only after she has pointed to the exact sentence in the essay. For B, let her use “I agree/disagree because…” to speak before writing. For D, give her a yellow highlighter and have her highlight the counterargument sentence in the paragraph you marked earlier. [[NEW]]This directly practices the benchmark steps: label the claim, find evidence, and identify an opposing view. [[/NEW]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA benchmarks: “Accurately annotate a given text for the prompt — labeled annotation focus, supporting detail, etc.” and “Independently find effective pieces of textual evidence to support the claim.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Sentence frame card: “I agree/disagree because _____.” Evidence box with one line only. [[/NEW]]

### 5) Independent Practice
- **Original step:** “Now it's your turn. Choose a social issue you care about—it could be about your school, your community, or something global. Write down: 1. The issue: _____ 2. Your position (what do you believe): _____ 3. Three reasons why you believe this: _____ 4. Evidence for each reason: _____”
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Give Jasmine a pre-set choice box with 3 possible social issues from today’s topic list or class examples, then have her choose one and complete the organizer with one reason and one evidence piece first. If she finishes, she adds reasons 2 and 3. [[NEW]]This keeps the standard intact but prevents overload from too many open choices and too much writing at once. [[/NEW]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA: “Write a claim that accurately answers each part of the question,” plus present level: “Low reading/writing stamina.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Choice box, 1-reason-at-a-time organizer, sentence starters for issue/position/reason/evidence. [[/NEW]]

### 6) Small group discussion
- **Original step:** “In small groups, share your chosen issues. Help each other by asking: - Is your position clear? - Are your reasons strong? - What evidence could support this better?”
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Keep Jasmine in the whole-group circle or her table group, and let her share using her organizer instead of reading full sentences aloud. Prompt her to answer only one peer question: “My position is _____.” [[NEW]]She likes peer talk and helps others, so this lets her participate without forcing extra reading. [[/NEW]]
- **IEP citation:** Student strength: “Enjoys peer talk.” Placement context from user: inclusion/whole group preferred.
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Organizer on desk and one share-out sentence frame. [[/NEW]]

### 7) Lesson close
- **Original step:** No separate close listed; pacing ends with small group discussion and peer feedback.
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[NEW]]End with a 20-second exit check: Jasmine points to one part of her organizer that shows her claim, then one piece of evidence. [[/NEW]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA benchmark: “Write a claim that accurately answers each part of the question” and “Independently find effective pieces of textual evidence to support the claim.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Exit check strip: “Claim / Evidence.” [[/NEW]]
