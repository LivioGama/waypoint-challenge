## PART A — Teacher Cheat Sheet

### Before the bell
- [[NEW]]Put Devon’s paper packet at his seat before class starts: the partner-reading questions, the four independent-practice lines, and the modified MCQs with the one-line “what this is really asking” note under each.[[/NEW]]
- [[CHANGED]]On the board, add the written directions that you would normally say out loud: “1) Read the model essay. 2) Answer the questions on paper. 3) Start the thesis planner. 4) Check in after each chunk.”[[/CHANGED]]
- [[NEW]]Place a red card on Devon’s desk and point to the warning-signs sheet in his folder during the first 30 seconds, so he sees the exact way to ask for a break without talking.[[/NEW]]

### Watch for
- [[NEW]]He goes quiet, stares at blank paper, or only writes his name when the class moves into “Independent Practice” and has to choose a social issue on the spot. That matches his pattern of blank/single-sentence work under whole-group pressure.[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]He keeps looking toward the door, rubs his hands, or stops copying after the model essay questions because the class is moving from reading to writing without a private check-in. That lines up with his anxiety trigger around performance and timed tasks.[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]He asks to get water, sharpen a pencil, or “just needs a minute” right when he has to write his position or evidence. That can be his early escape move before he uses the red-card break.[[/NEW]]

### If they disengage
Use this exact 2-step script:
1. [[NEW]]Point to the red card and say, “Use your silent signal or choose box breathing for 60 seconds.”[[/NEW]]
2. [[NEW]]After the break, hand back to the same sentence stem: “Write just your issue and your position. You only need the first line right now.”[[/NEW]]

[[NEW]]This matches Goal 1 — Anxiety self-management: “Independently identify an early warning sign from the personal warning-signs sheet and deploy a taught coping strategy (box breathing, 5-4-3-2-1 grounding, red-card break, request co-regulation) BEFORE escalation.” It also matches the accommodation “5-minute breaks on request, no explanation required” and “Red-card 'silent signal' on desk; teacher acknowledges with nod, no verbal exchange.”[[/NEW]]

### Praise specifically
- [[NEW]]“You picked a clear issue and your position is already strong — that’s the exact kind of thesis work strong writers do.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“I like how you wrote your idea first instead of freezing; that helped you stay in the lesson with the rest of us.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You matched a reason to evidence — that’s the part of persuasive writing that makes an argument convincing.”[[/NEW]]

## PART B — Modified Lesson Plan

### Block 1: Intro to persuasive writing
- **Original step:** “Today we're going to start by looking at what makes an argument persuasive. Turn to your neighbor and talk about a time when someone convinced you to change your mind about something. What did they say? How did they convince you?”
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Let Devon keep his response written instead of turning and talking. Give him a 2-sentence response frame on paper: “Someone convinced me to change my mind about ___ because ___. They were convincing when ___.” If he wants to share, he can raise his hand, but no speaking is required.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** [[NEW]]Goal 2 — Written expression — at grade level: “Written response accepted in place of any oral response.” Also Goal 1 — Anxiety self-management: “request co-regulation” before escalation.[[/NEW]]
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Replace the oral share with a one-half-page written quick-write and the two-sentence frame above.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]At the very start of class, during the first 5 minutes.[[/NEW]]

### Block 2: Read the model essay
- **Original step:** “Now, let's look at a model persuasive essay. Pay attention to: The clear statement of what the writer believes; The reasons and evidence they provide; How they handle disagreement.”
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[SAME]]Read the model essay with the class.[[/SAME]] [[NEW]]As you read, hand Devon a highlighter only for these three jobs: underline the thesis, circle one reason, box one counterargument sentence. He marks on the page while the class listens, so he is active without extra talking.[[/NEW]]
- **IEP citation:** [[NEW]]Goal 2 — Written expression — at grade level: “Use a written planning template (claim → 3 evidence → conclusion) before drafting.”[[/NEW]]
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]A 3-symbol annotation key: underline = thesis, circle = reason, box = counterargument.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]During the 5–20 minute whole-group model essay segment.[[/NEW]]

### Block 3: Partner reading questions
- **Original step:** “Work with a partner to analyze the essay. Answer these questions together: 1. What is the writer trying to convince us of? 2. What evidence do they use? 3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?”
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Let Devon answer the same three questions in writing on his paper before any partner talk happens. Then, if he chooses, he can compare answers with a partner by pointing to his written response instead of speaking aloud.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** [[NEW]]Present Levels — ELA: “Strongly prefers writing over speaking.” Goal 2 — Written expression — at grade level: “Respond in writing to teacher feedback within 48 hours, 4/5 instances.”[[/NEW]]
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Printed answer boxes under each question plus a yes/no choice line for question 3: “I think it is convincing / not convincing because ___.”[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]During the 20–30 minute partner-reading block, before any group discussion.[[/NEW]]

### Block 4: During-reading questions
- **Original step:** “A. Think & Share… B. Turn & Talk… C. Write… D. Find Evidence…”
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Keep A, B, C, and D on a single page with the directions already written out. Ask Devon to complete C and D first, then choose either to nod or point for A and B instead of speaking. If he needs a reset, he uses the red card before moving to the next question.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** [[NEW]]Accommodations — “Directions given in writing whenever oral directions are given,” “Written response accepted in place of any oral response,” and “Red-card 'silent signal' on desk; teacher acknowledges with nod, no verbal exchange.”[[/NEW]]
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]One-page during-reading sheet with checkboxes for A–D and space to copy one evidence sentence.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]Right after the model essay is read, during the question set.[[/NEW]]

### Block 5: Independent Practice
- **Original step:** “Now it's your turn. Choose a social issue you care about—it could be about your school, your community, or something global. Write down: 1. The issue; 2. Your position; 3. Three reasons why you believe this; 4. Evidence for each reason.”
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Keep the same task and standard, but narrow the start so he writes one line at a time in the room with everyone else: first issue, then position, then reason 1, reason 2, reason 3. Give him a choice bank of 6 school/community/global topics so he is not starting from a blank page. Check in after each line is completed.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** [[NEW]]Goal 2 — Written expression — at grade level: “Use a written planning template (claim → 3 evidence → conclusion) before drafting.” Also present levels: “In whole-group setting, often submits blank paper or single sentence.”[[/NEW]]
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]A choice bank of six topics plus a planning organizer with five boxes: issue, position, reason 1, reason 2, reason 3, evidence lines.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]At minute 30, when the class moves into independent practice.[[/NEW]]

### Block 6: Student-led discussion
- **Original step:** “In small groups, share your chosen issues. Help each other by asking: Is your position clear? Are your reasons strong? What evidence could support this better?”
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Let Devon stay with the group, but he can show his organizer instead of speaking. If he wants to contribute, he can read one sentence he already wrote. Do not cold-call him.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** [[NEW]]Accommodations — “No cold-calling — Devon raises hand or signals to participate” and “Written response accepted in place of any oral response.”[[/NEW]]
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]A small sticky-note version of the three discussion questions so he can point to a response instead of answering from memory.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]During the 40–45 minute peer feedback block.[[/NEW]]

### Block 7: Multiple-choice practice questions
- **Original step:** “Multiple-Choice Practice Questions 1–4.”
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Give the same four questions with a one-line gloss under each item that says what the question is really asking. He circles A, B, C, or D on paper, then writes one short reason only if needed for check-in.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** [[NEW]]Goal 2 — Written expression — at grade level: “complete and submit grade-level written responses” and accommodations: “Typed response accepted for any handwritten task.”[[/NEW]]
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Modified MCQ sheet with reduced vocabulary load, answer bubbles, and the “what this question is really asking” line.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]If you use the practice questions during or after the lesson, hand the modified sheet at that moment.[[/NEW]]

### Block 8: Closure / check-out
- **Original step:** Not explicitly stated, but the lesson ends after small-group discussion and peer feedback.
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[NEW]]End with a 20-second written exit check: “What is your issue? What is your position?” He turns in the paper or types it in a shared doc before leaving.[[/NEW]]
- **IEP citation:** [[NEW]]Goal 2 — Written expression — at grade level: “Submit final draft (typed) within agreed extended deadline, 4/5 assignments.”[[/NEW]]
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Two-line exit ticket in writing; typed option if needed.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]In the last 2 minutes of class, before dismissal.[[/NEW]]

## PART C — Modified Student Materials

### 1) Partner Reading Questions
[[CHANGED]]
**Directions:** Read the model essay. Answer in writing.

1. What is the writer trying to convince us of?  
**What this question is really asking:** [[NEW]]What is the writer’s claim or thesis?[[/NEW]]

2. What evidence do they use?  
**What this question is really asking:** [[NEW]]What facts, examples, or quotes support the claim?[[/NEW]]

3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?  
**What this question is really asking:** [[NEW]]Do the reasons and evidence make the argument strong?[[/NEW]]

**Sentence stems:**  
- The writer is trying to convince us that ___.  
- One piece of evidence they use is ___.  
- I think the argument is convincing / not convincing because ___.  
[[/CHANGED]]

### 2) During-Reading Questions
[[CHANGED]]
**A. Think & Share**  
After reading the introduction, what do you think this essay will argue?  
**What this question is really asking:** [[NEW]]What do you think the thesis will be?[[/NEW]]

**B. Turn & Talk**  
With your neighbor, discuss: Do you agree or disagree with the writer's main point so far?  
**What this question is really asking:** [[NEW]]Do you think the writer’s claim is strong so far?[[/NEW]]

**C. Write**  
Jot down one piece of evidence the writer uses and whether it supports their claim.  
**What this question is really asking:** [[NEW]]Find one fact/example and say if it helps the argument.[[/NEW]]

**D. Find Evidence**  
Highlight one sentence where the writer acknowledges a different point of view.  
**What this question is really asking:** [[NEW]]Find the counterargument sentence.[[/NEW]]

**Checklist:**  
- [ ] I underlined the thesis.  
- [ ] I wrote one piece of evidence.  
- [ ] I found the counterargument sentence.

### 4) Multiple-Choice Practice Questions
[[CHANGED]]
1. Which of the following best describes a thesis statement?  
A. A question about the topic  
B. A clear statement of the author's position or belief  
C. A list of all the evidence in the essay  
D. The conclusion of the essay  
**What this question is really asking:** [[NEW]]Which answer tells the reader the writer’s claim?[[/NEW]]  
**Answer: B**

2. What is a counterargument?  
A. A reason that supports the main claim  
B. An opposing point of view that the author addresses  
C. The evidence used in the essay  
D. The introduction to the essay  
**What this question is really asking:** [[NEW]]Which answer means the writer shows the other side?[[/NEW]]  
**Answer: B**

3. If you were writing a persuasive essay to your school principal about extending lunch time, which evidence would be MOST credible?  
A. "My friend thinks we need more time to eat"  
B. A study showing that students with longer lunch breaks have better afternoon attendance  
C. "Food tastes better when you eat it slowly"  
D. "I saw someone rushing through lunch yesterday"  
**What this question is really asking:** [[NEW]]Which choice uses the most trustworthy proof?[[/NEW]]  
**Answer: B**

4. Which sentence is the best thesis statement for a persuasive essay?  
A. "Many students struggle with homework."  
B. "Our school should require students to have one free period for homework instead of study hall."  
C. "Some people like homework, and some people don't."  
D. "Homework is a topic that people discuss."  
**What this question is really asking:** [[NEW]]Which sentence clearly takes a side?[[/NEW]]  
**Answer: B**
