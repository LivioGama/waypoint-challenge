## Part B — Modified Lesson Plan

### 1) Whole Class Reading: hook + model essay
- **Original step:** “Turn to your neighbor and talk about a time when someone convinced you to change your mind… Now, let's look at a model persuasive essay.”
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Keep him in whole group. Give him TTS before the turn-and-talk starts, and tell him he can answer orally to his partner instead of reading the first paragraph on his own. After the model essay is played/read aloud, ask him to point to the sentence that shows the writer’s position. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech: **“With speech-to-text, produces on-grade ideas”** and Goal 3 — Self-advocacy: **“Independently request and set up his accommodations (TTS, headphones, extra time)”**; accommodation **“Text-to-speech for any text > 1 paragraph”**; present level **“Comprehension drops sharply on multi-clause sentences.”**
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Audio read of the model essay + a sticky note with one focus question: “What is the writer’s position?” [[/NEW]]

### 2) Model essay analysis
- **Original step:** “Pay attention to: the clear statement, reasons and evidence, how they handle disagreement.”
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Give him a 3-box note catcher: Claim / Evidence / Counterargument. He fills it in by speaking into speech-to-text or by saying answers to a partner while the class discusses. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech: **“Dictate a complete claim sentence answering the prompt”** and **“Dictate two pieces of supporting evidence with paragraph references.”**
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Three-box organizer with starter labels already printed and one example under each label. [[/NEW]]

### 3) Partner Reading
- **Original step:** “Work with a partner to analyze the essay… What is the writer trying to convince us of? What evidence do they use? Is their argument convincing?”
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Keep the partner reading in the main room. Marcus listens with TTS, and his partner reads the questions aloud while he answers using short oral responses or speech-to-text. Do not ask him to read aloud first. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Present level of behavior: **“Avoidance of any task that requires reading aloud unprompted. Loses confidence quickly when called on cold.”** Accommodation **“Oral responses accepted in place of writing for comprehension checks.”**
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Question strip with one question per line and a one-sentence answer frame: “The writer is trying to convince us that ___.” [[/NEW]]

### 4) During Reading Questions A–D
- **Original step:**  
  A. Think & Share  
  B. Turn & Talk  
  C. Write  
  D. Find Evidence
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Use the same question set, but give him the question one at a time. For C, he may dictate his answer instead of writing it. For D, give him a highlighter or digital highlight tool and point him to the paragraph with the counterargument sentence. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech: **“Dictate two pieces of supporting evidence with paragraph references”**; accommodation **“Speech-to-text for written responses > 2 sentences”**; present level **“With speech-to-text, produces on-grade ideas.”**
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]A-C-D mini card with one box at a time, plus a highlighted copy of paragraph 8 for the counterargument hunt. [[/NEW]]

### 5) Independent Practice: choose a social issue and plan
- **Original step:** “Choose a social issue you care about… 1. The issue 2. Your position 3. Three reasons 4. Evidence for each reason”
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Keep the same task, but narrow the front-end choice: he picks from 3 teacher-approved social issues first, then names his position. After that, he fills in two reasons and two evidence ideas now; the third reason/evidence can be added later during the writing draft. He uses speech-to-text for the response. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech: **“Produce a 5-sentence on-grade analytical paragraph using speech-to-text + spell check”** and benchmark **“Dictate a complete claim sentence answering the prompt.”** Present level: **“ideas are strong.”**
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Choice board with 3 topic options, claim stem, and a 2-reason organizer. [[/NEW]]

### 6) Student-Led Discussion
- **Original step:** “In small groups, share your chosen issues… Is your position clear? Are your reasons strong? What evidence could support this better?”
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]Keep him in the small group. Give him a role card: “claim checker.” He reads or hears one peer’s claim and says whether it is clear, then gives one follow-up question. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Present level of behavioral: **“Generally engaged and well-liked. Responds well to choice (which scaffold to use) and to being treated as a capable thinker.”**
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Role card with two stems: “Your claim is clear because ___.” / “You could add evidence from ___.” [[/NEW]]

### 7) MCQ practice
- **Original step:** Multiple-choice questions 1–4
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[CHANGED]]He answers the same 4 questions, but they are read aloud through TTS and each choice is broken onto its own line. He circles or says the answer, then gives one short reason. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Assessment accommodation **“Text-to-speech for ELA reading passages and questions”** and present level **“Comprehension drops sharply on multi-clause sentences.”**
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]One-question-at-a-time answer sheet with reduced line clutter. [[/NEW]]

### 8) End of lesson share-out
- **Original step:** “End with small group discussions where students can get feedback on their positions and evidence.”
- **Modified step for Marcus:** [[SAME]]He stays in the group share-out. [[/SAME]] [[NEW]]Before he speaks, he looks at his organizer and gives either a spoken claim or one dictated sentence. [[/NEW]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — Self-advocacy: **“Politely decline unprompted oral reading without disengaging.”** Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech.
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Completed organizer from Independent Practice + one exit stem: “My claim is ___ because ___.” [[/NEW]]

## Part C — Modified Student Materials

### 1) Partner Reading Questions
#### Q1
[[CHANGED]]**1. What is the writer trying to convince us of?**[[/CHANGED]]

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “What is the writer’s main claim?”[[/NEW]]

**Answer choice / expected answer:** The writer is trying to convince us that our school should have a longer lunch period because students need more time to eat, socialize, and recharge.

---

#### Q2
[[CHANGED]]**2. What evidence do they use?**[[/CHANGED]]

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which facts or examples support the claim?”[[/NEW]]

**Answer choice / expected answer:** They mention that students rush through lunch, that social time is important for mental health, and that teachers report students are less focused in afternoon classes when they haven't had adequate break time.

---

#### Q3
[[CHANGED]]**3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?**[[/CHANGED]]

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Do the reasons and evidence make sense?”[[/NEW]]

**Answer choice / expected answer:** Responses may vary, but look for students to acknowledge the strengths of the evidence and whether they found it persuasive.

---

### 2) Multiple-Choice Practice Questions
#### Q1
[[CHANGED]]**1. Which of the following best describes a thesis statement?**[[/CHANGED]]

A. A question about the topic  
B. A clear statement of the author's position or belief  
C. A list of all the evidence in the essay  
D. The conclusion of the essay  

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which answer means the main claim?”[[/NEW]]

**Answer:** B

---

#### Q2
[[CHANGED]]**2. What is a counterargument?**[[/CHANGED]]

A. A reason that supports the main claim  
B. An opposing point of view that the author addresses  
C. The evidence used in the essay  
D. The introduction to the essay  

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which answer names the other side of the argument?”[[/NEW]]

**Answer:** B

---

#### Q3
[[CHANGED]]**3. If you were writing a persuasive essay to your school principal about extending lunch time, which evidence would be MOST credible?**[[/CHANGED]]

A. "My friend thinks we need more time to eat"  
B. A study showing that students with longer lunch breaks have better afternoon attendance  
C. "Food tastes better when you eat it slowly"  
D. "I saw someone rushing through lunch yesterday"  

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which choice is the strongest, most trustworthy evidence?”[[/NEW]]

**Answer:** B

---

#### Q4
[[CHANGED]]**4. Which sentence is the best thesis statement for a persuasive essay?**[[/CHANGED]]

A. "Many students struggle with homework."  
B. "Our school should require students to have one free period for homework instead of study hall."  
C. "Some people like homework, and some people don't."  
D. "Homework is a topic that people discuss."  

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which sentence clearly says what the writer believes?”[[/NEW]]

**Answer:** B
