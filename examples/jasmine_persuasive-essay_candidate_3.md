## Part B — Modified Lesson Plan

### 1) Opening / hook
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Today we're going to start by looking at what makes an argument persuasive. Turn to your neighbor and talk about a time when someone convinced you to change your mind about something.”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Keep her in the whole-group turn-and-talk, but give her one choice card on the desk: “A time I changed my mind was… because…” If she freezes, point to the card and let her read the first six words aloud.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **Goal 3 — ELA**: “**Independently answer literal and inferential comprehension questions or writing prompts that meet grade-level rubrics**”; **student**: “**Likes participating in ELA**” and “**enjoys peer talk**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]One choice-card stem, no extra worksheet. [[NEW]]Student stays in partner talk. [[NEW]]Teacher points to the stem instead of re-explaining the whole question.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]During the first 0–5 minutes, right after the hook is read aloud.[[/NEW]]

### 2) Model essay introduction
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Now, let's look at a model persuasive essay. Pay attention to the clear statement of what the writer believes, the reasons and evidence they provide, and how they handle disagreement.”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Hand her the pre-highlighted model and ask her to trace with a finger: 1) the claim, 2) one evidence sentence, 3) the counterargument sentence. She says each one in her own words to you or a partner.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **present_levels.ela**: “**Needs significant adult support to break down vocabulary, sentence structures, key ideas, and to organize thinking**”; **Goal 3 — ELA** benchmark: “**Accurately annotate a given text for the prompt — labeled annotation focus, supporting detail, etc.**”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Pre-highlighted copy, 3-labeled annotation marks, and a 3-box mini note strip: claim / evidence / counterargument.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]During 5–20 minutes, while the class reads the model essay.[[/NEW]]

### 3) Partner reading questions
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Work with a partner to analyze the essay. Answer these questions together: 1. What is the writer trying to convince us of? 2. What evidence do they use? 3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Keep the same three questions, but give her a half-sheet with the questions rewritten in shorter words and one line under each that says what it means. Let her answer by circling, underlining, or speaking first, then writing one short phrase.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **Goal 3 — ELA**: “**Independently answer literal and inferential comprehension questions**”; **present_levels.ela**: “**Difficulty accessing grade-level texts**” and “**reading-homework accuracy 0–50% independent**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Short-question version + “what it means” line + answer box with space for 1 phrase or 1 sentence.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]During 20–30 minutes, while the partner-reading questions are done.[[/NEW]]

### 4) During-reading prompts
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“After reading the introduction, what do you think this essay will argue?” / “Do you agree or disagree with the writer's main point so far?” / “Jot down one piece of evidence the writer uses and whether it supports their claim.” / “Highlight one sentence where the writer acknowledges a different point of view.”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Use only the first and third prompts for her, and put them on a sticky note: “What will this essay argue?” and “What evidence did you spot?” She marks the introduction and one evidence sentence in the text instead of answering all four prompts.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **Goal 3 — ELA** benchmark: “**Write a claim that accurately answers each part of the question**” and “**Independently find effective pieces of textual evidence to support the claim**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Two sticky-note prompts, highlighter, and a 2-column box: “claim” / “evidence.”[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]During 20–30 minutes, immediately after the intro is read and during partner analysis.[[/NEW]]

### 5) Independent practice: choose a social issue
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Now it's your turn. Choose a social issue you care about—it could be about your school, your community, or something global. Write down: 1. The issue 2. Your position 3. Three reasons why you believe this 4. Evidence for each reason.”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Keep the same task, but give her a topic bank with 4 pre-selected issues and one finished example for the “issue / position / reasons / evidence” layout. She chooses one topic, then fills in only one reason and one piece of evidence first; after a check-in, she adds the second and third reasons.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **Goal 3 — ELA**: “**Write a claim that accurately answers each part of the question**”; **present_levels.ela**: “**Needs significant adult support to break down vocabulary, sentence structures, key ideas, and to organize thinking**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Topic bank, example organizer, and a 4-box organizer with one box partially modeled.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]During 30–40 minutes, at the start of independent practice.[[/NEW]]

### 6) Small-group discussion / peer feedback
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“In small groups, share your chosen issues. Help each other by asking: Is your position clear? Are your reasons strong? What evidence could support this better?”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Keep her with classmates in the same room. Give her one feedback card with two stems: “Your position is clear because…” and “One stronger evidence idea is…” She reads one stem and uses it once.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **student**: “**enjoys peer talk**”; **placement**: “**Full Inclusion Program**”; **Goal 3 — ELA** benchmark: “**Independently find effective pieces of textual evidence to support the claim**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Two feedback stems only; no extra role cards or separate group.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]During 40–45 minutes, in the class discussion block.[[/NEW]]

### 7) Teacher response to struggle
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]Teacher circulates and supports students as needed.[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Jasmine:** [[CHANGED]]Check her first at minute 12, again right after topic choice, and again before she starts the evidence box. At each check, ask her to point to the claim line before talking.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** **present_levels.ela**: “**Needs frequent reminders to track text, annotate, reread when independent**”; **Goal 1 — Counseling / Self-regulation** benchmark: “**Recognize and describe physical cues of being triggered**” and “**Independently request or initiate a calming strategy when dysregulated**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]A consistent point-to-text routine and a 10-second pause cue.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]During model reading, topic choice, and the first evidence entry.[[/NEW]]

## Part C — Modified Student Materials

### 1) Partner Reading Questions — Jasmine version
[[NEW]]**Directions:** Read the model essay with your partner. Answer each question with a word, phrase, or short sentence. You may circle, underline, or say your answer first.[[/NEW]]

1. [[NEW]]Which of the following best describes a thesis statement?[[/NEW]]  
   A. A question about the topic  
   B. A clear statement of the author's position or belief  
   C. A list of all the evidence in the essay  
   D. The conclusion of the essay  
   **Answer: B**  
   [[NEW]]What this question is really asking: Which choice means “the writer’s main position”?[[/NEW]]

2. [[NEW]]What is a counterargument?[[/NEW]]  
   A. A reason that supports the main claim  
   B. An opposing point of view that the author addresses  
   C. The evidence used in the essay  
   D. The introduction to the essay  
   **Answer: B**  
   [[NEW]]What this question is really asking: Which choice means “the other side”?[[/NEW]]

3. [[NEW]]If you were writing a persuasive essay to your school principal about extending lunch time, which evidence would be MOST credible?[[/NEW]]  
   A. “My friend thinks we need more time to eat”  
   B. A study showing that students with longer lunch breaks have better afternoon attendance  
   C. “Food tastes better when you eat it slowly”  
   D. “I saw someone rushing through lunch yesterday”  
   **Answer: B**  
   [[NEW]]What this question is really asking: Which choice sounds like trustworthy proof, not just an opinion?[[/NEW]]

4. [[NEW]]Which sentence is the best thesis statement for a persuasive essay?[[/NEW]]  
   A. “Many students struggle with homework.”  
   B. “Our school should require students to have one free period for homework instead of study hall.”  
   C. “Some people like homework, and some people don't.”  
   D. “Homework is a topic that people discuss.”  
   **Answer: B**  
   [[NEW]]What this question is really asking: Which sentence clearly tells the writer’s position?[[/NEW]]
