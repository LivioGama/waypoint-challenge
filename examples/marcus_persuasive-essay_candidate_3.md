## PART B — Modified Lesson Plan

### Block 1: Whole Class Reading
- Original step: “Today we're going to start by looking at what makes an argument persuasive. Turn to your neighbor and talk about a time when someone convinced you to change your mind about something.”
- Modified step for Marcus: [[CHANGED]]Before the turn-and-talk, briefly whisper the two key vocabulary words “persuasive” and “claim” and point to the definitions on the board while Marcus follows with TTS on the directions if needed. Keep him in the whole-class discussion, and let him answer orally or with a quick typed note to his partner. [[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech: “**Produce a 5-sentence on-grade analytical paragraph using speech-to-text + spell check**”; Goal 3 — Self-advocacy: “**Independently request and set up his accommodations (TTS, headphones, extra time)**.”
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Tiny vocab card with “persuasive = tries to convince” and “claim = what you believe.” [[/NEW]]

### Block 2: Model Essay Preview
- Original step: “Now, let's look at a model persuasive essay. Pay attention to: - The clear statement of what the writer believes - The reasons and evidence they provide - How they handle disagreement”
- Modified step for Marcus: [[CHANGED]]Give Marcus the model essay with TTS running. Ask him to track only three things by highlighting or saying them aloud: the claim, one reason, and one counterargument sentence. [[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: Present levels — ELA: “**With text-to-speech, comprehension ~grade level on the same text**”; Goal 1 — Reading fluency benchmark: “**Re-read for fluency after first cold read**.”
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Three-color highlighter key: claim / reasons / counterargument. [[/NEW]]

### Block 3: Partner Reading
- Original step: “Work with a partner to analyze the essay. Answer these questions together: 1. What is the writer trying to convince us of? 2. What evidence do they use? 3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?”
- Modified step for Marcus: [[CHANGED]]Keep the partner reading, but give Marcus the modified question sheet with the one-sentence gloss under each question. Let him answer by speaking first, then capture the answer in writing or speech-to-text. [[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech: “**Dictate two pieces of supporting evidence with paragraph references**”; Assessment accommodations: “**Speech-to-text for open-response items**.”
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Partner-reading question card with answer frame: “The writer is trying to convince us that ____. One piece of evidence is ____. This is convincing because ____.” [[/NEW]]

### Block 4: During Reading Questions A-D
- Original step: “A. Think & Share… B. Turn & Talk… C. Write… D. Find Evidence”
- Modified step for Marcus: [[CHANGED]]For A and B, let Marcus answer orally first. For C, have him dictate one evidence sentence into speech-to-text instead of handwriting. For D, point him to highlight one counterargument sentence, then have him say why it counts as a different point of view. [[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: Present levels — ELA: “**ideas are strong; spelling and mechanics are well below grade level**”; Assessment accommodations: “**Speech-to-text for open-response items**” and “**Oral responses accepted in place of writing for comprehension checks**.”
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Sentence stem strip: “The evidence is ___. It supports the claim because ___.” / “This sentence shows the other side because ___.” [[/NEW]]

### Block 5: Independent Practice — Choose a Social Issue
- Original step: “Now it's your turn. Choose a social issue you care about—it could be about your school, your community, or something global. Write down: 1. The issue: 2. Your position (what do you believe): 3. Three reasons why you believe this: 4. Evidence for each reason:”
- Modified step for Marcus: [[CHANGED]]Keep the same task and expectations, but give Marcus a pre-made organizer with four boxes and sentence starters. He chooses one issue, says his claim aloud first, then uses speech-to-text to fill the Position box and at least two Reasons/Evidence boxes. [[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech: “**Dictate a complete claim sentence answering the prompt**” and “**Dictate two pieces of supporting evidence with paragraph references**.”
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Main-idea organizer with boxes: Issue / Position / Reason 1 + evidence / Reason 2 + evidence / Reason 3 + evidence. [[/NEW]]

### Block 6: Student-Led Discussion
- Original step: “In small groups, share your chosen issues. Help each other by asking: - Is your position clear? - Are your reasons strong? - What evidence could support this better?”
- Modified step for Marcus: [[CHANGED]]Keep Marcus in the small group. Give him one speaking job: read or say just his claim sentence and one reason, then let peers ask one follow-up question. Do not ask him to read his draft aloud. [[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: Goal 3 — Self-advocacy benchmark: “**Politely decline unprompted oral reading without disengaging**”; Present levels — behavioral: “**Becomes self-deprecating about reading in front of peers; will refuse to read aloud**.”
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Discussion card with two optional responses: “My claim is ___.” “One reason is ___.” [[/NEW]]

### Block 7: Lesson Close / Pacing Check
- Original step: “Suggested Pacing… Minutes 40-45: Small group discussion and peer feedback”
- Modified step for Marcus: [[SAME]]Keep him in the small-group feedback time, but ask for one exact next step: add one piece of evidence or revise one claim sentence. [[/SAME]]
- IEP citation: Goal 2 — Written expression with assistive tech: “**Edit dictated text for missing punctuation and homophone errors**.”
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]End-of-lesson checklist: Claim sentence complete? Evidence added? Punctuation checked? [[/NEW]]

### 2) During Reading Questions

#### C. Write
[[CHANGED]]Jot down one piece of evidence the writer uses and whether it supports their claim.[[/CHANGED]]

[[CHANGED]]Highlight one sentence where the writer acknowledges a different point of view.[[/CHANGED]]
