# Tomorrow's Lesson — Elena (Grade 7)

## PART A — Teacher Cheat Sheet

### Before the bell
- [[NEW]]Put Elena’s desk copy on her table before students enter: visual schedule, 1-page “clue → guess → check” organizer, and the modified question sheet for Independent Practice.[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]Seat Elena away from the door, sharpener, and flicker zone; place her noise-cancelling headphones where she can grab them without asking.[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]Write the three lesson targets on the board in plain English: “What is community?”, “Find the main idea,” and “Use text clues to explain your answer.”[[/NEW]]

### Watch for
- [[NEW]]She starts answering literally without using the text’s big idea, like focusing only on Newcastle or the word “group,” because the article’s abstract meaning and perspective-taking are the hard part for her. [[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]She goes quiet, scripts, or keeps rereading the same line when the lesson gets into “shared story,” “identity-forming narrative,” or the partner discussion about communities she belongs to. [[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]She looks at the room more than the page, covers her ears, or stops writing during partner talk, especially in the discussion block after independent work. [[/NEW]]

### If they disengage
Use this exact 2-step script:
1. [[CHANGED]]“Elena, check your cue card: do you need headphones, a 3-minute break, or help with the next question?”[[/CHANGED]]
2. [[CHANGED]]“Show me one clue from the text, then say: ‘I think this means ___ because ___.’”[[/CHANGED]]

### Praise specifically
- [[NEW]]“You found the clue and the meaning—your answer stayed with the text and did not get stuck on one word.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You used the sentence frame to explain your thinking clearly. That is strong academic reading.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You stayed in the whole-group lesson and kept working even when the idea was abstract. That shows strong self-management.”[[/NEW]]
