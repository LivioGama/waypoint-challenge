## PART A — Teacher Cheat Sheet

### Before the bell
- [[NEW]]Put Elena’s visual schedule at her desk and circle the three lesson moves she’ll do today: model essay, partner questions, independent topic choice. This takes under a minute and matches Goal 1 — Pragmatic communication / Self-regulation: "Visual schedule posted at desk" and Goal 3 — ELA — Figurative language and inference: "With sentence-frame organizer, accuracy rises to 75–85%."[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]Tape a one-page response card on her desk with the exact frame: “The text says ___ → I think this means ___ → because ___” and a short note box for “issue / position / reason / evidence.” This is a 60-second setup and matches Goal 3 benchmark: "Apply the 'clue → guess → check' rule card" plus the accommodation "Sentence-frame organizers for inference and figurative-language items."[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]Prewrite one example topic she can borrow if she freezes: “school lunch time,” “cell phone policy,” or “homework load.” Put it on an index card next to the lesson materials. This is tied to the lesson’s "Choose a social issue you care about" step and the present level: "Writing is grammatically clean but lacks audience awareness; tends toward expository even when prompt asks for narrative."[[/NEW]]

### Watch for
- [[NEW]]If the class turn-and-talk at line 88 starts with “a time someone convinced you to change your mind,” Elena may take that literally and answer with a fact instead of a personal example. That’s a cue to step in before she shuts down, because Goal 1 says she needs help with pragmatic breakdowns and present levels note she "repairs pragmatic breakdowns ... with adult coaching only."[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]During the model essay talk, if the room gets noisy and she starts staring at the page, tapping, or going quiet, she may be hitting overload. That matches Goal 1 baseline: she "requests sensory break independently in 1/5 opportunities" and present levels say sensory triggers include group transitions.[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]If the partner questions at lines 96–99 or small-group share at lines 121–125 move too fast, she may answer with the first obvious answer and miss the “why.” That fits Goal 3 baseline: "Literal comprehension at G8; inferential at G5."[[/NEW]]

### If they disengage
- [[NEW]]Step 1: Quietly point to the desk cue card and say, “Use your I need a minute choice: headphones, deep pressure, or safe-space pass.” This uses the exact taught regulation options named in Goal 1 — "headphones, sensory break, safe-space pass, deep pressure."[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]Step 2: After she chooses, say, “Come back to this line: The text says ___ → I think this means ___ → because ___.” This matches Goal 3 benchmark: "Apply the 'clue → guess → check' rule card" and fits the lesson’s model-essay and question tasks.[[/NEW]]

### Praise specifically
- [[NEW]]“You named the writer’s position and matched it to evidence — that’s exactly how a persuasive thesis works.” Tie this to Partner Reading Question 1 and Goal 3’s text-evidence frame.[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You used the sentence frame to explain what the counterargument means instead of just copying the sentence.” Tie this to During Reading Question D and Goal 3 benchmark: "Distinguish literal vs figurative meaning..."[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You stayed with the group and finished your issue, position, reason, and evidence boxes.” Tie this to Independent Practice lines 115–119 and Goal 1’s self-regulation/participation skills.[[/NEW]]

## PART B — Modified Lesson Plan

### Whole Class Reading
- **Original step:** “Today we're going to start by looking at what makes an argument persuasive. Turn to your neighbor and talk about a time when someone convinced you to change your mind about something.”
- [[CHANGED]]**Modified step for Elena:** Start her with the desk card: “Use one example from a book, a show, school, or real life where someone changed an opinion,” and let her answer in writing first, then share one sentence with her assigned partner. Keep her with whole group; do not pull her out. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 1 — Pragmatic communication / Self-regulation: "Written response accepted in place of oral participation for whole-group share-outs"; Goal 3 — ELA — Figurative language and inference: "With sentence-frame organizer, accuracy rises to 75–85%."
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** Response card with “It changed my mind when ___ because ___.” plus a visual cue for “example from book / show / school / real life.”

### Model Essay Preview
- **Original step:** “Now, let's look at a model persuasive essay. Pay attention to: the clear statement of what the writer believes; the reasons and evidence they provide; how they handle disagreement.”
- [[CHANGED]]**Modified step for Elena:** Give her a highlighter strip that matches three colors to the three things she must track: thesis, evidence, counterargument. Ask her to mark only one example of each in the model essay while the teacher reads aloud. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA — Figurative language and inference: "Baseline: Literal comprehension at G8; inferential at G5" and benchmark "Generate one inference per paragraph during guided reading, with text evidence cited."
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** Three-color highlight key with labels: thesis / evidence / counterargument.

### Partner Reading
- **Original step:** “Work with a partner to analyze the essay. Answer these questions together: 1. What is the writer trying to convince us of? 2. What evidence do they use? 3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?”
- [[CHANGED]]**Modified step for Elena:** Keep the questions the same, but give her the answer frame under each one and ask her partner to read her response after she writes it. She should answer in full sentences, not single words. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA — Figurative language and inference: "With sentence-frame organizer, accuracy rises to 75–85%" and benchmark "Identify character motivation using a 3-step text-evidence frame, 4/5 prompts."
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** Partner-reading sheet with sentence stems:
  - “The writer is trying to convince us that ___.”
  - “One piece of evidence is ___.”
  - “This is convincing/not convincing because ___.”

### During Reading Questions A–D
- **Original step:** “A. Think & Share... B. Turn & Talk... C. Write... D. Find Evidence...”
- [[CHANGED]]**Modified step for Elena:** For A, B, C, and D, let her circle the exact sentence or paragraph in the model essay before answering. She should use the clue → guess → check card for A and D, and the teacher should prompt her to point to the line before talking. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA — Figurative language and inference: benchmark "Apply the 'clue → guess → check' rule card to 5 idioms per week, 80% accuracy" and "Distinguish literal vs figurative meaning of a target sentence in a grade-level passage, 4/5 items."
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** Small sticky notes labeled A/B/C/D and a clue → guess → check mini-card.

### Independent Practice
- **Original step:** “Now it's your turn. Choose a social issue you care about—it could be about your school, your community, or something global. Write down: 1. The issue... 2. Your position... 3. Three reasons... 4. Evidence for each reason.”
- [[CHANGED]]**Modified step for Elena:** Give her a narrowed choice bank of 3–4 grade-level topics and a fill-in organizer with the same four boxes. She chooses one issue, writes her position, then adds one reason and one piece of evidence at a time instead of trying to do all four boxes at once. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA — Figurative language and inference: "With sentence-frame organizer, accuracy rises to 75–85%" and present level "Writing is grammatically clean but lacks audience awareness"; lesson line 157 says, "Support students in finding credible evidence for their claims."
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** Topic choice bank + four-box organizer with sentence stems:
  - “The issue I chose is ___.”
  - “I believe ___.”
  - “One reason is ___.”
  - “Evidence for this reason is ___.”

### Student-Led Discussion
- **Original step:** “In small groups, share your chosen issues. Help each other by asking: Is your position clear? Are your reasons strong? What evidence could support this better?”
- [[CHANGED]]**Modified step for Elena:** Keep her in the group with her assigned role as recorder or materials manager, and give her one question card she can read out loud or hand to a peer: “What evidence best supports your claim?” [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 1 — Pragmatic communication / Self-regulation: "Assigned role in every group task (recorder, timekeeper, materials manager)" and "Written response accepted in place of oral participation for whole-group share-outs."
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** Role card + one discussion card with one question and one reply stem: “The best evidence is ___ because ___.”

### Suggested Pacing
- **Original step:** “Minutes 0-5... 5-20... 20-30... 30-40... 40-45...”
- [[CHANGED]]**Modified step for Elena:** Keep the same pacing, but build in a 30-second check after the model essay and again right before independent practice to confirm she has the thesis/evidence/counterargument labels and her topic choice written down. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 1 — Pragmatic communication / Self-regulation: "Visual schedule posted at desk" and "Advance notice ... of schedule changes"; Goal 3 baseline notes "With sentence-frame organizer, accuracy rises to 75–85%."
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** Quick desk check points at minutes 20 and 30.

### Whole text support notes
- **Original step:** “Students may choose any social issue they care about... Support students in finding credible evidence for their claims.”
- [[SAME]]**Same for Elena:** Keep the content and the grade-level standard. Let her choose a real issue, but hand her one credible source example on the topic if she gets stuck, so she still has to judge evidence rather than invent it. [[/SAME]]
- **IEP citation:** Lesson note line 157: "Support students in finding credible evidence for their claims."; Goal 3 benchmark: "Generate one inference per paragraph during guided reading, with text evidence cited."
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** One sample source card and a “credible / not credible” quick sort.

## PART C — Modified Student Materials

### 1) Partner Reading Questions
[[CHANGED]]
**1. What is the writer trying to convince us of?**  
A. A question about the topic  
B. A clear statement of the author's position or belief  
C. A list of all the evidence in the essay  
D. The conclusion of the essay  
**Answer: B**

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “What is the thesis — the writer’s main claim?”[[/NEW]]

[[CHANGED]]
**2. What is a counterargument?**  
A. A reason that supports the main claim  
B. An opposing point of view that the author addresses  
C. The evidence used in the essay  
D. The introduction to the essay  
**Answer: B**

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “What opposite idea does the writer bring up and respond to?”[[/NEW]]

[[CHANGED]]
**3. If you were writing a persuasive essay to your school principal about extending lunch time, which evidence would be MOST credible?**  
A. "My friend thinks we need more time to eat"  
B. A study showing that students with longer lunch breaks have better afternoon attendance  
C. "Food tastes better when you eat it slowly"  
D. "I saw someone rushing through lunch yesterday"  
**Answer: B**

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which choice sounds trustworthy because it is based on research?”[[/NEW]]

[[CHANGED]]
**4. Which sentence is the best thesis statement for a persuasive essay?**  
A. "Many students struggle with homework."  
B. "Our school should require students to have one free period for homework instead of study hall."  
C. "Some people like homework, and some people don't."  
D. "Homework is a topic that people discuss."  
**Answer: B**

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which sentence clearly says what the writer wants to argue?”[[/NEW]]
