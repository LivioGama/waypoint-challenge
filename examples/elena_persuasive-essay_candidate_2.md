# Tomorrow’s Lesson — Elena (Grade 7)

## Part A — Teacher Cheat Sheet

### Before the bell
- [[NEW]]Put Elena’s sentence-frame strip on her desk: “The text says ___ → I think this means ___ → because ___,” plus the 4-question partner sheet with the answer choices boxed in bold. [[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]Print one copy of the Independent Practice page with extra space under each line for Elena to type or write, and add a tiny reminder at the top: “Use a clear position + 3 reasons + evidence.” [[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]Mark Elena’s seat where she can see the model essay, the board, and your face, and place headphones at the desk before class starts. [[/NEW]]

### Watch for
- [[NEW]]She may answer the partner-reading questions literally, especially “What is the writer trying to convince us of?” by repeating a detail instead of naming the claim. That matches her IEP pattern: “inferential and figurative-language items… are answered literally.” [[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]She may stall when the lesson shifts from reading to choosing her own social issue, because her writing tends to stay “expository even when prompt asks for narrative,” so she may list facts instead of taking a position. [[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]She may get quiet or fidgety during the small-group share if the room gets noisy; her IEP notes headphones are allowed and that overload signs show up before she can independently ask. [[/NEW]]

### If they disengage
Use this exact 2-step script from Goal 1 — Pragmatic communication / Self-regulation:
1. “[[NEW]]Check your cue card: are you noticing overload? If yes, say, ‘I need a minute.’[[/NEW]]”
2. “[[NEW]]Pick one regulation tool now: headphones or a 3-minute safe-space pass. Then come back and do the next one question with me.[[/NEW]]”

### Praise specifically
- [[NEW]]“You named the writer’s position, not just the topic—that’s a strong thesis move.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You used evidence that actually matches the claim. That’s the kind of precise thinking persuasive writers need.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You stayed with the question even when it asked for a different point of view. That shows real control of the argument.”[[/NEW]]

## Part B — Modified Lesson Plan

### 1) Whole Class Reading
- **Original step:** “Today we're going to start by looking at what makes an argument persuasive. Turn to your neighbor and talk about a time when someone convinced you to change your mind about something. What did they say? How did they convince you?”
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Before turn-and-talk, quietly point to the board sentence frame: “Someone convinced me when they ___ because ___.” Elena uses that frame instead of answering from memory alone. Keep her in whole group and let her speak or write one sentence to a partner, not round-robin.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA “Figurative language and inference”; benchmark: “Distinguish literal vs figurative meaning of a target sentence in a grade-level passage, 4/5 items.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Mini response strip with the sentence frame and the words “what happened / why it worked.”[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]Right after the teacher asks the opening turn-and-talk question, before partner talk starts.[[/NEW]]

### 2) Model Essay Preview
- **Original step:** “Now, let's look at a model persuasive essay. Pay attention to: The clear statement of what the writer believes; The reasons and evidence they provide; How they handle disagreement.”
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Give Elena a 3-color highlight key: yellow = thesis, blue = reasons/evidence, green = counterargument. She tracks the model essay with the colors while the class listens/read-alouds, then points to each color once with a partner.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Present Levels — “Literal comprehension at G8; inferential at G5”; Goal 3 benchmark: “Generate one inference per paragraph during guided reading, with text evidence cited.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Color key card with the three labels and a blank “evidence” box for quick notes.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]During the model essay read-aloud, before the class discussion starts.[[/NEW]]

### 3) Partner Reading Questions
- **Original step:** “Work with a partner to analyze the essay. Answer these questions together: 1. What is the writer trying to convince us of? 2. What evidence do they use? 3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?”
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Keep the same questions, but give Elena the answer stem card: “The writer is trying to convince us that ___. The evidence is ___. I think it is convincing/not convincing because ___.” Have her point to the exact line in the essay before she answers.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA “Figurative language and inference”; present level: “With sentence-frame organizer, accuracy rises to 75–85%.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Partner question card with the answer stems printed under each question and a “point to the line” reminder.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]At the start of Partner Reading, before she and her partner answer Question 1.[[/NEW]]

### 4) During Reading Questions A–D
- **Original step:**  
  A. “Think & Share: After reading the introduction, what do you think this essay will argue?”  
  B. “Turn & Talk: With your neighbor, discuss: Do you agree or disagree with the writer's main point so far?”  
  C. “Write: Jot down one piece of evidence the writer uses and whether it supports their claim.”  
  D. “Find Evidence: Highlight one sentence where the writer acknowledges a different point of view.”
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Keep A–D, but hand Elena a one-page “clue → guess → check” sheet. She fills in only one item per question: one clue, one guess, one check. For D, she highlights first, then says in writing, “This is the counterargument because ___.”[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 benchmark: “Apply the ‘clue → guess → check’ rule card to 5 idioms per week, 80% accuracy.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]One-page cue sheet with three boxes per question and the word bank: clue, guess, check, counterargument.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]While the class is working through questions A–D, starting with A.[[/NEW]]

### 5) Independent Practice
- **Original step:** “Now it's your turn. Choose a social issue you care about—it could be about your school, your community, or something global. Write down: 1. The issue: ___ 2. Your position (what do you believe): ___ 3. Three reasons why you believe this: ___ 4. Evidence for each reason: ___”
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Give Elena a narrowed choice box with three issue categories instead of unlimited topics: school, community, or global. Then keep the same task, but require her to write the position as a claim sentence first, before listing reasons. She may type her answers.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Present Levels — “writing is grammatically clean but lacks audience awareness; tends toward expository even when prompt asks for narrative”; accommodation: “Permission to type rather than handwrite when fine-motor fatigue triggers regulation issues.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Topic choice box + claim-first organizer with spaces for “I believe ___ because ___, ___, and ___.”[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]As soon as the class transitions to Independent Practice, before students begin choosing topics.[[/NEW]]

### 6) Student-Led Discussion
- **Original step:** “In small groups, share your chosen issues. Help each other by asking: Is your position clear? Are your reasons strong? What evidence could support this better?”
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[CHANGED]]Keep Elena in the group, but assign her the role of “evidence finder.” She must read one reason aloud and point to one piece of evidence from her organizer before the group moves on. If the room gets loud, she may put on headphones and still stay in the group.[[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Accommodation: “Noise-cancelling headphones permitted at all times without staff prompting”; Placement: inclusion/whole group.
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Role card that says “Evidence Finder” with two prompts: “My reason is ___” and “My evidence is ___.”[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]Right before small-group discussion begins, during the role assignment.[[/NEW]]

### 7) Closing / Collection
- **Original step:** not explicitly stated beyond pacing.
- **Modified step for Elena:** [[NEW]]Collect Elena’s organizer before dismissal and quickly check for these 4 items: issue, position, 3 reasons, 1 piece of evidence per reason. If one box is empty, ask her to finish that one box before leaving.[[/NEW]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 3 — ELA benchmark: “Generate one inference per paragraph during guided reading, with text evidence cited.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Single-check exit organizer with 4 boxes matching the independent practice page.[[/NEW]]
- **When:** [[NEW]]Last 2 minutes of class, after small-group discussion and before dismissal.[[/NEW]]

## Part C — Modified Student Materials

### 1) Partner Reading Questions
[[CHANGED]]
**1. What is the writer trying to convince us of?**  
A. The writer’s main point or claim  
B. A detail from the essay  
C. The conclusion only  
D. A random fact

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “What does the writer want the reader to believe?”[[/NEW]]

**2. What evidence do they use?**  
A. Facts, examples, or quotes from the essay  
B. A joke  
C. A personal opinion with no proof  
D. The title

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “What proof does the writer give?”[[/NEW]]

**3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?**  
A. Yes, because the evidence matches the claim  
B. No, because the essay has no topic  
C. Yes, because it is long  
D. No, because it has a title

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Does the evidence actually support the claim?”[[/NEW]]
[[/CHANGED]]

**Answer key:** 1-A, 2-A, 3-A

### 2) During Reading Questions A–D
[[CHANGED]]
**A. Think & Share**  
After reading the introduction, what do you think this essay will argue?

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “What claim will the writer make?”[[/NEW]]

**B. Turn & Talk**  
With your neighbor, discuss: Do you agree or disagree with the writer's main point so far?

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Do you think the writer’s claim makes sense so far?”[[/NEW]]

**C. Write**  
Jot down one piece of evidence the writer uses and whether it supports their claim.

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which detail is proof, and does it help the claim?”[[/NEW]]

**D. Find Evidence**  
Highlight one sentence where the writer acknowledges a different point of view.

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Where does the writer show the other side?”[[/NEW]]
[[/CHANGED]]

- B: “I agree/disagree because ___.”
