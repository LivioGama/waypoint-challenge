# Tomorrow's Lesson — Devon (Grade 7)

## Part A — Teacher Cheat Sheet

### Before the bell
- [[NEW]]Put Devon’s modified sheet on his desk before class: the 4 multiple-choice questions, the independent practice organizer, and a sticky note with the co-regulation script. [[/NEW]] This takes under 60 seconds and keeps him from starting with a blank page, which is the main problem in whole-group writing.
- [[CHANGED]]Set his seat near the door and leave the red-card signal visible on the desk, plus one highlighter and one pencil. [[/CHANGED]] He already has the IEP support to leave without speaking, so make the signal obvious from the start.
- [[NEW]]Circle the “today’s stop point” on his organizer: “I will finish the first paragraph here before any break option.” [[/NEW]] That matches his written-expression benchmark and helps him stay in gen ed for the first chunk.

### Watch for
- [[NEW]]He goes quiet or stares at the blank page right when the lesson moves from the model essay to “Now it’s your turn” in Independent Practice. [[/NEW]] That’s the spot where his anxiety usually shows up as avoidance.
- [[NEW]]He starts over-erasing, rubbing his hands, or asking “Do I have to pick one?” when the issue choice feels too open-ended in the Independent Practice list. [[/NEW]] He does better when the task is narrowed quickly.
- [[NEW]]He stops marking text during “Find Evidence” or skips the counterargument question because the vocabulary gets heavy. [[/NEW]] The lesson asks for analysis, but the wording can trigger shutdown if it feels too dense.

### If they disengage
Use the IEP strategy already named: the red-card silent signal and co-regulation script.

1. [[CHANGED]]Point to the red-card on his desk, nod, and say: “Use the card if you need it. First, choose one issue from the list.” [[/CHANGED]]
2. [[CHANGED]]If he signals, hand him the script card: “Breathe 4 times. Circle one issue. Start with the first line only.” [[/CHANGED]]

### Praise specifically
- [[NEW]]“You wrote a clear position from the model essay and matched it to your own topic — that’s strong argument thinking.” [[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You kept going even when the question asked for evidence, and you picked a source that actually fits the claim.” [[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You finished part of the planner in the room with everyone else, which is exactly the kind of start we want.” [[/NEW]]

## Part B — Modified Lesson Plan

### Block 1: Whole Class Reading / Launch
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Today we're going to start by looking at what makes an argument persuasive. Turn to your neighbor and talk about a time when someone convinced you to change your mind about something.”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Let Devon answer in writing on a sticky note instead of talking to a neighbor, then have him hand it to you or place it face-up on his desk. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 2 — Written expression — at grade level: “**Respond in writing to teacher feedback within 48 hours, 4/5 instances.**” Accommodations: “**Written response accepted in place of any oral response**” and “**No cold-calling — Devon raises hand or signals to participate**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Sticky note response starter: “A time someone convinced me was when ______ because ______.” [[/NEW]]

### Block 2: Model Essay Preview
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Now, let's look at a model persuasive essay. Pay attention to: - The clear statement of what the writer believes - The reasons and evidence they provide - How they handle disagreement”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Give Devon a 3-box bookmark for the model essay: Box 1 = thesis, Box 2 = evidence, Box 3 = counterargument. Tell him to mark one word or phrase in each box while you read aloud. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 2 — Written expression — at grade level: “**Use a written planning template (claim → 3 evidence → conclusion) before drafting, 4/5 assignments.**” Present Levels ELA: “**Written expression on grade level when not under time/performance pressure**” and “**Strong written work when given choice of topic and quiet setting.**”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]3-box bookmark with labels: “What the writer believes / Proof / What someone else might say.” [[/NEW]]

### Block 3: Partner Reading
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Work with a partner to analyze the essay. Answer these questions together: 1. What is the writer trying to convince us of? 2. What evidence do they use? 3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Let Devon answer the 3 questions in writing on his copy first; then, if he chooses, he can show the partner one answer instead of speaking the whole response. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Accommodations: “**Written response accepted in place of any oral response**” and “**Strongly prefers writing over speaking**.” Present Levels ELA: “**produces multi-paragraph essays with clear thesis and evidence when working alone in resource room**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Answer box starter under each question: “The writer is trying to convince us that ____.” / “One piece of evidence is ____.” / “This is convincing because ____.” [[/NEW]]

### Block 4: During Reading Questions A–D
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“A. Think & Share… B. Turn & Talk… C. Write… D. Find Evidence”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Have Devon do A, C, and D in writing only; skip Turn & Talk unless he raises his hand to share. Give him a highlighter to mark the counterargument sentence in question D. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Accommodations: “**No cold-calling — Devon raises hand or signals to participate**,” “**Written response accepted in place of any oral response**,” and “**Errors not marked in red ink**.” Goal 1 — Anxiety self-management: “**deploy a taught coping strategy (box breathing, 5-4-3-2-1 grounding, red-card break, request co-regulation) BEFORE escalation**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Mini question strip: “What will this essay argue?” “What proof did the writer use?” “Which sentence shows the other side?” [[/NEW]]

### Block 5: Independent Practice — choose a social issue
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Now it's your turn. Choose a social issue you care about—it could be about your school, your community, or something global. Write down: 1. The issue 2. Your position 3. Three reasons why you believe this 4. Evidence for each reason”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Before he starts writing, narrow the choice to one of three teacher-approved topics you prewrite on the board, then have him pick one and complete only the first paragraph in class before any break or move option. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 2 — Written expression — at grade level: “**Complete first paragraph in gen ed setting before optional move to alternate setting, 3/5 assignments.**” Present Levels ELA: “**In whole-group setting, often submits blank paper or single sentence.**” Placement: “**Full Inclusion Program with alternate setting for assessments**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Topic-choice strip with 3 options + planning box: “Issue / My position / 3 reasons / evidence.” [[/NEW]]

### Block 6: Small Group Discussion and Peer Feedback
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“In small groups, share your chosen issues. Help each other by asking: Is your position clear? Are your reasons strong? What evidence could support this better?”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]Let Devon stay in the whole-group room and read one sentence from his planner to the teacher instead of joining a group share unless he volunteers. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Accommodations: “**No cold-calling — Devon raises hand or signals to participate**” and “**Written response accepted in place of any oral response**.” Present Levels behavioral/social-emotional: “**engages well with trusted adults**” and “**recovers quickly when given space**.”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Feedback card for teacher use: “Your position is clear because ____.” “One reason I noticed is ____.” “One piece of evidence that fits is ____.” [[/NEW]]

### Block 7: Lesson Wrap / Pacing
- **Original step:** [[SAME]]“Minutes 0-5: Introduction and definition of persuasive writing… Minutes 40-45: Small group discussion and peer feedback”[[/SAME]]
- **Modified step for Devon:** [[CHANGED]]At minute 30, check his first paragraph before the class moves on. If he has the paragraph started, he keeps working in class; if not, give the red-card break option and return target: finish one sentence after the break. [[/CHANGED]]
- **IEP citation:** Goal 1 — Anxiety self-management: “**use a taught coping strategy… BEFORE escalation**” and benchmark “**Use red-card silent signal independently when warning signs appear, 4/5 opps.**” Goal 2 — Written expression — at grade level: “**Submit final draft (typed) within agreed extended deadline, 4/5 assignments.**”
- **Exact materials swap or scaffold:** [[NEW]]Minute-30 check box on his paper: “Started first paragraph: yes / not yet.” [[/NEW]]

## Part C — Modified Student Materials

### 1) Multiple-Choice Practice Questions
[[CHANGED]]**1. Which of the following best describes a thesis statement?**  
A. A question about the topic  
B. A clear statement of the author's position or belief  
C. A list of all the evidence in the essay  
D. The conclusion of the essay  
**Answer: B**  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which answer names the main claim the writer wants you to believe?”[[/NEW]]

[[CHANGED]]**2. What is a counterargument?**  
A. A reason that supports the main claim  
B. An opposing point of view that the author addresses  
C. The evidence used in the essay  
D. The introduction to the essay  
**Answer: B**  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which answer means ‘the other side’ that the writer responds to?”[[/NEW]]

[[CHANGED]]**3. If you were writing a persuasive essay to your school principal about extending lunch time, which evidence would be MOST credible?**  
A. “My friend thinks we need more time to eat”  
B. A study showing that students with longer lunch breaks have better afternoon attendance  
C. “Food tastes better when you eat it slowly”  
D. “I saw someone rushing through lunch yesterday”  
**Answer: B**  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which choice is strongest because it uses proof you can trust?”[[/NEW]]

[[CHANGED]]**4. Which sentence is the best thesis statement for a persuasive essay?**  
A. “Many students struggle with homework.”  
B. “Our school should require students to have one free period for homework instead of study hall.”  
C. “Some people like homework, and some people don't.”  
D. “Homework is a topic that people discuss.”  
**Answer: B**  
[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: “Which sentence clearly takes a side and tells the reader what should happen?”[[/NEW]]

### 2) Open-Response / Writing Prompt
