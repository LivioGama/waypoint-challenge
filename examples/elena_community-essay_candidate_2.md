# Tomorrow's Lesson — Elena (Grade 7)

## PART A — Teacher Cheat Sheet

### Before the bell
- [[NEW]]Put Elena’s visual mini-schedule on her desk: **Warm-up → Read → Questions → Independent Practice → Partner talk → Done**.[[/NEW]]  
- [[NEW]]Circle and pre-mark the 4 words that may trip her up in this text: **community, narrative, identity-forming, normative**.[[/NEW]]  
- [[NEW]]Place her modified question sheet, sentence-frame organizer, and headphones at her seat before students enter.[[/NEW]]

### Watch for
- [[NEW]]She starts reading every line literally but misses the author’s point, especially on “shared story,” “identity-forming narrative,” or the phrase “community is not, in and of itself, morally praiseworthy.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]She stops writing or stares at the page after the Newcastle example or the two bullet lists, because the text has a lot of abstract language and nested ideas.[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]She gets rigid about the partner talk if her role is unclear or if the question feels open-ended, which can lead to silence, scripting, or refusal.[[/NEW]]

### If they disengage
1. [[CHANGED]]Point to her desk schedule and say: “We are at the next box. Put on your headphones or use the 3-minute break if you need it.”[[/CHANGED]]  
2. [[CHANGED]]Say: “Tell me one sentence using the frame: **The text says ___ → I think it means ___ → because ___**.” Then wait 30 seconds.[[/CHANGED]]

### Praise specifically
- [[NEW]]“You found the clue in the text and matched it to the author’s definition. That was precise reading.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You used the sentence frame to explain an abstract idea clearly. That is strong academic writing.”[[/NEW]]
- [[NEW]]“You stayed with the whole-group lesson and handled the partner task with your role. That shows good self-management.”[[/NEW]]

## PART B — Modified Lesson Plan

### 1) Intro Slide Deck — 5 min
- Original step: Guide students through the slide deck to introduce them to the reading lesson and skill focus.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]Start by pointing to the posted visual schedule and saying the lesson goal in one sentence: “Today we will figure out the central idea of the article and the details that support it.” Hand her the pre-marked vocabulary strip so she can track the 4 key words while the class talks.[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Goal 3 — ELA — Figurative language and inference: “Identify character motivation using a 3-step text-evidence frame, 4/5 prompts” and “Distinguish literal vs figurative meaning of a target sentence in a grade-level passage, 4/5 items.” Present level: “Literal comprehension is strong; inferential comprehension is at G5.”[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Visual schedule, 4-word vocabulary strip, and a one-line lesson goal card.[[/NEW]]

### 2) Vocabulary / Purpose for Reading — embedded in intro
- Original step: Let’s pronounce these words together as a class.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]When the class reads the vocabulary aloud, give Elena a small card with plain-English meanings next to the words, and have her whisper-read the words once with the group instead of being put on the spot.[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Accommodation: “Pre-teach figurative language and idioms before any text containing them” and “Directions given in writing AND orally; no idioms in directions.” Present level: “Precise vocabulary” is a strength.[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Vocabulary card with simple definitions and one example sentence for each word.[[/NEW]]

### 3) Whole Class Reading — Paragraphs 1-2
- Original step: Whole class reading.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]Before the class starts Paragraph 1, give Elena 30 seconds to preview the two anchor questions: “What claim does Lowe make?” and “What is the relationship between the claim and the bulleted list?” Then ask her to underline only the sentence that states the claim.[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Accommodation: “No ‘cold call’ on inference questions without 30-second think-time and access to organizer.” Goal 3 benchmark: “Apply the ‘clue → guess → check’ rule card to 5 idioms per week, 80% accuracy.”[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Two-question preview card and a highlight strip for the claim sentence.[[/NEW]]

### 4) During Reading Questions — Paragraphs 1-2
- Original step: A. Think & Share; B. Write; C. Turn & Talk.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]Keep her in whole group for A and B. For B, let her answer on a printed organizer instead of speaking first: “The claim is ___ / The bulleted list shows ___.” For C, assign her the role of **recorder** so she writes one group idea instead of generating multiple ideas on the spot.[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Accommodation: “Written response accepted in place of oral participation for whole-group share-outs” and “Assigned role in every group task (recorder, timekeeper, materials manager).” Present level: “Prefers parallel play and structured cooperative tasks with assigned roles.”[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Paragraphs 1-2 response frame and a role card labeled “Recorder.”[[/NEW]]

### 5) Partner Reading — Paragraphs 3-7
- Original step: Partner reading.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]Keep partner reading, but give Elena the first turn to read only Paragraph 3 aloud quietly to the partner, then have the partner read Paragraph 4. Ask her to stop after Paragraph 5 and complete one organizer row: “The text says ___ / This shows ___ about community.”[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Accommodation: “Written directions given in writing AND orally” and “Directions given in writing AND orally; no idioms in directions.” Present level: “Decoding and fluency well above grade level” and “with visual scaffold accuracy rises to 75–85% on inference items.”[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Mini organizer with one row per paragraph chunk and a stop-point after Paragraph 5.[[/NEW]]

### 6) During Reading Questions — Paragraphs 3-7
- Original step: A. Write; B. Turn & Talk.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]For the Turn & Talk in B, give her the exact talk stem on a strip: “One community I am part of is ___ because ___.” If she does not want to speak, she may hand the strip to the partner after writing it.[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Accommodation: “Written response accepted in place of oral participation for whole-group share-outs.” Goal 1 — Pragmatic communication / Self-regulation: “use a taught repair phrase… in 3 of 5 pragmatic-breakdown opportunities.”[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Talk stem strip and a write-first option.[[/NEW]]

### 7) Whole Class Reading — Paragraph 8
- Original step: Whole class reading.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]Pause after the first bullet in Paragraph 8 and ask the class to compare it to Paragraph 2. Give Elena a two-column note catcher labeled “Paragraph 2 says” and “Paragraph 8 proves.” She fills in one item in each column before reading on.[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Goal 3 — ELA — Figurative language and inference: “Generate one inference per paragraph during guided reading, with text evidence cited.” Present level: “Inferential comprehension at G5.”[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Two-column note catcher tied to the author’s claim-testing move.[[/NEW]]

### 8) During Reading Questions — Paragraph 8
- Original step: A. Turn & Talk; B. Write.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]Keep the Turn & Talk, but pre-assign Elena’s role as “evidence finder.” Her job is to point to one sentence that shows how the definition works, then she writes the sentence number in the organizer instead of explaining at length.[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Accommodation: “Written response accepted in place of oral participation for whole-group share-outs.” Present level: “Exceptional pattern recognition” and “accurate citations.”[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Sentence-number tracker and evidence finder role card.[[/NEW]]

### 9) Whole Class Reading — Paragraphs 10-11
- Original step: Read the ending and discuss why the title matters.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]Before reading Paragraph 11, tell her the exact focus question in writing: “How does the author connect community to social change?” After the paragraph, ask her to underline the sentence that answers the title question and circle one phrase that shows importance.[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Goal 3 — ELA — Figurative language and inference: “Identify character motivation using a 3-step text-evidence frame” and “Distinguish literal vs figurative meaning…” Present level: “Literal recall is near perfect.”[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Underline/circle task card and the title-question strip.[[/NEW]]

### 10) Independent Practice — Multiple Choice Questions
- Original step: Students complete multiple choice questions.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]Give Elena the modified MCQ page with one-sentence “what this question is really asking” lines under each item. Let her answer in the margin or on the page, and allow her to keep the passage open while answering.[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Accommodation: “No ‘cold call’ on inference questions without 30-second think-time and access to organizer” and “Extra time (1.5x) on assessments containing inference or open-response items.” Present level: “With visual scaffold accuracy rises to 75–85% on inference items.”[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Modified MCQ page with simplified vocabulary glosses under each question.[[/NEW]]

### 11) Independent Practice — Short Response
- Original step: Students complete a short answer prompt.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]Give Elena the prompt with a sentence-stem scaffold and a tiny checklist tied to Goal 3. Require 2 text details, not a longer response. Let her type her answer if handwriting slows her down.[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Accommodation: “Permission to type rather than handwrite when fine-motor fatigue triggers regulation issues” and “Sentence-frame organizers for inference and figurative-language items.” Goal 3 benchmark: “Identify character motivation using a 3-step text-evidence frame, 4/5 prompts.”[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Short-response scaffold with sentence frames and a two-detail checklist.[[/NEW]]

### 12) Student-Led Discussion — 5 min
- Original step: Discuss the questions with a partner. Record both of your answers.
- Modified step for Elena: [[CHANGED]]Keep her in the partner discussion, but put the questions on a card at her desk first. She answers question 1 by writing a concrete example of courteous behavior, and question 2 by using the stem “A person can belong without agreeing because ___.”[[/CHANGED]]
- IEP citation: [[NEW]]Goal 1 — Pragmatic communication / Self-regulation: “use a taught repair phrase… in 3 of 5 pragmatic-breakdown opportunities.” Accommodation: “Written response accepted in place of oral participation for whole-group share-outs.” Present level: “Prefers parallel play and structured cooperative tasks with assigned roles.”[[/NEW]]
- Exact materials swap or scaffold: [[NEW]]Partner discussion card with one written stem per question.[[/NEW]]

### 1) Multiple-Choice Questions with reduced vocabulary load

#### Question 1
**Original question:**  
Which best describes the author’s main purpose in writing the article? [RI.6]

A. to illustrate how important it is to be part of multiple communities  
B. to demonstrate the way community has shaped the author's life  
C. to describe the life of a person who lives in Newcastle  
D. to give a clear and useful definition of community  

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: What is the author mainly trying to do in this article?[[/NEW]]

#### Question 2
**Original question:**  
Which piece of evidence best illustrates the central idea of the article? [RI.1]

A. “So, one of the communities that I consider myself to be part of is the community based around the city of Newcastle.” (Paragraph 5)  
B. “It is this choice to participate in the making and remaking of these stories about the city that makes me part of the community of Newcastle.” (Paragraph 7)  
C. “Community is a group of people who share a story that is so important to them that it defines an aspect of who they are.” (Paragraph 9)  
D. “Social change requires that we rewrite our communal narratives.” (Paragraph 11)

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: Which quote best proves the article’s main idea?[[/NEW]]

#### Question 3
**Original question:**  
Reread the excerpt from paragraph 5:  
“The manifestations of this are that I take pride in showing people around the city. I feel slighted when people say horrible things about it. I feel at home whenever I hear a Geordie (Newcastle) accent (despite not having one myself).”  
What is the best meaning of the word “slighted” as it is used in the excerpt? [RI.4]

A. bored  
B. insulted  
C. motivated  
D. recognized  

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: What does “slighted” mean in this sentence?[[/NEW]]

#### Question 4
**Original question:**  
How do paragraphs 5-7 help the reader understand the author’s central idea? [RI.2]

A. by explaining why community is neither good not bad  
B. by describing life for members of the Newcastle community  
C. by giving an example of a community that is part of the author’s story  
D. by illustrating how the Newcastle community has many different members  

[[NEW]]What this question is really asking: How do these paragraphs help prove the author’s main point?[[/NEW]]

**Original prompt:**  
You have just read “What is community and why is it important?” by Toby Lowe. Explain what Lowe means when he says a community is “a group of people who share an identity-forming narrative.” [RI.2]
