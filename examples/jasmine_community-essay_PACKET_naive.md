## Teacher Packet: Adapted Lesson for Jasmine Regina Bailey  
**Lesson:** *What is “community” and why is it important?* by Toby Lowe  
**Grade:** 7 ELA  
**Student:** Jasmine Regina Bailey  
**Primary needs:** Attention, task initiation, stamina, focus, study skills, adaptability; benefits from structure, repetition, and low-friction response supports.

---

# 1) Student Snapshot for Instruction

### Strengths to leverage
- Warm, cooperative, and can make thoughtful comments when calm
- Likes participating in ELA and peer talk
- Responds well to specific praise
- Enjoys creative tasks and connecting ideas to real life

### Likely barriers in this lesson
- Long informational text may lead to fatigue or avoidance
- May struggle to sustain attention through multiple sections
- Inferential and analysis questions may feel overwhelming
- Writing the short response independently may trigger shutdown
- May need help identifying the central idea and relevant evidence

### Instructional priorities for this lesson
1. Keep reading chunks short and purposeful  
2. Reduce cognitive load with guided notes and sentence frames  
3. Check for understanding frequently  
4. Build success through oral rehearsal before writing  
5. Use structured choices and planned breaks to prevent shutdown

---

# 2) Lesson Goal for Jasmine

By the end of the lesson, Jasmine will:
- identify the central idea of the text with support,
- select at least 2 important details that develop it,
- explain the meaning of the phrase **“identity-forming narrative”** using a sentence frame or organizer,
- participate in partner discussion using academic vocabulary.

---

# 3) Key Vocabulary Support

### Focus words
- **community**
- **identity**
- **narrative**
- **belonging**
- **shared**
- **central idea**
- **evidence**

### Student-friendly definitions
- **community** = a group of people connected by place, interests, or shared story
- **identity** = who a person is
- **narrative** = a story
- **belonging** = feeling accepted and included
- **central idea** = the main point of a text
- **evidence** = details from the text that support an answer

### Quick vocabulary support strategy
Before reading, preview 3 words only:
- community
- narrative
- belonging

Then add the others during reading as needed.

# 4) Recommended Lesson Adaptations

## A. Intro Slide Deck — 5 min
### Adaptation
- Give Jasmine a **guided notes sheet** with 3 blanks:
  1. Today’s text is about __________.
  2. The author’s main idea may be __________.
  3. One word I need to know is __________.

### Teacher move
- Use a brief “notice and predict” discussion:
  - “What do you think community means?”
  - “What might a shared story be?”
- Allow oral answers first.

### Support for engagement
- Seat Jasmine near teacher/front of room.
- Give a clear start cue: “We’re doing 3 parts today: read, talk, write.”

---

## B. During Reading — adapt the whole-class reading
Because Jasmine has low stamina and gets overwhelmed by independent reading, use **chunked reading with frequent pauses**.

### Best modality for Jasmine
- Whole class reading aloud or teacher-supported partner reading
- Short sections only
- Optional teacher read-aloud for dense paragraphs

### Structure
Read one section at a time:
1. Paragraphs 1–2
2. Paragraphs 3–7
3. Paragraph 8
4. Paragraphs 9–11

After each chunk:
- stop,
- ask one guided question,
- have Jasmine underline or highlight one key detail.

### Support tools
- Copy of teacher notes
- Highlighter or pencil
- Small graphic organizer:  
  **Claim / Important detail / What it shows**

### What to say
- “Let’s find the one sentence that matters most.”
- “Tell me the idea in your own words.”
- “You do not need to answer everything perfectly. Just get the main idea.”

# 5) Modified During Reading Questions

Below are adapted prompts for Jasmine with reduced language load.

## Paragraphs 1–2
**Original task:** What claim does Lowe make about the word “community”?  
**Adapted prompt:**  
**What does Lowe say about the word community?**  
Sentence frame:  
- “Lowe says community is __________.”

**Expected response support:**  
- It has power / can mean different things / people do not always define it clearly.

---

## Paragraph 2
**Original task:** Relationship between claim and bulleted list  
**Adapted prompt:**  
**Why does Lowe make a list?**  
Sentence frame:  
- “He makes a list to show __________.”

**Expected response support:**  
- He is explaining what a good definition of community should include.

---

## Paragraph 2
**Original task:** Summarize three key traits of a community  
**Adapted prompt:**  
**Choose 2 traits of community from the list.**  
Possible choices:
- people feel they belong
- communities can be good or bad
- communities shape beliefs
- people can belong to more than one community

---

## Paragraphs 3–7
**Original task:** How does Newcastle support the definition?  
**Adapted prompt:**  
**How does Newcastle help prove Lowe’s idea?**  
Sentence frame:  
- “Newcastle helps prove the idea because __________.”

**Expected response support:**  
- He feels pride, follows local traditions, and sees the city as part of his story.

---

## Paragraph 8
**Original task:** Why another bulleted list?  
**Adapted prompt:**  
**Why does the author test his idea again?**  
Sentence frame:  
- “He tests the idea again to show that __________.”

**Expected response support:**  
- His definition works for all the traits of community.

---

## Paragraphs 10–11
**Original task:** Find evidence that answers both questions  
**Adapted prompt:**  
**Highlight 1 sentence that tells what community is.**  
**Highlight 1 sentence that tells why community matters.**

### Suggested evidence for Jasmine to notice
- “Community is a group of people who share a story…”
- “Social change requires that we rewrite our communal narratives.”

# 6) Independent Practice Adaptations

## Multiple Choice
Jasmine may complete:
- all 4 questions, **or**
- 2 questions selected by teacher if fatigue is high, with quality over quantity

### Supports
- Read each question aloud
- Cover answer choices until she explains her thinking
- Use elimination: “Which one is definitely not right?”

### Extra scaffolds
For each item, prompt:
- “What is the question asking?”
- “Where did we see that in the text?”
- “Which answer matches the author’s main idea?”

---

## Short Response Prompt
**Original prompt:** Explain what Lowe means when he says a community is “a group of people who share an identity-forming narrative.”

### Adapted version for Jasmine
Use this version if writing stamina is low:

**Explain what Lowe means by “identity-forming narrative.” Use 2 details from the text.**

### Sentence frame option
- “Lowe means that a community is __________.”
- “One detail from the text is __________.”
- “Another detail is __________.”
- “This shows that __________.”

### Optional word bank
community, story, identity, belonging, shared, people, narrative, pride

### If writing is very difficult
Allow Jasmine to:
- orally dictate her response to teacher or aide
- then copy a shortened version
- or complete a fill-in-the-blank response

# 8) Student-Led Discussion Adaptation

Jasmine benefits from peer talk, but needs structure.

### Best setup
- Partner with a patient, supportive peer
- Give Jasmine the first turn only after quick rehearsal
- Keep discussion to 1 or 2 questions if needed

### Sentence starters
1. “In my community, courteous behavior is __________.”
2. “I think people can/can’t be part of a community without agreeing because __________.”
3. “One benefit of belonging to more than one community is __________.”

### Teacher support
- Preteach one response before partner talk
- Remind her she can use notes
- Circulate and prompt her back if she freezes

# 9) Behavior and Engagement Supports

Because frustration can lead to withdrawal, use proactive supports:

### Before the lesson
- Provide a clear agenda
- Give her a role: highlighter, note-finder, discussion starter
- Set a goal: “Find 2 key ideas and one quote.”

### During the lesson
- Use “pause, plan, proceed”
- Check in every 5–7 minutes
- Offer a quick break before shutdown occurs
- Praise specific effort:
  - “You stayed with that paragraph.”
  - “Good job finding evidence.”
  - “I like how you tried that sentence frame.”

### If she shuts down
- Reduce demands immediately
- Offer a choice:
  - “Do you want to underline or point to the answer?”
  - “Do you want to say it first or write it first?”
- Return to one small task only
