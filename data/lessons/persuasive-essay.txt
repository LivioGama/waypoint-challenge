How do I facilitate this lesson?

Notes to Teachers
This lesson focuses on helping students develop and defend a position on a social issue they care about. The writing process emphasizes evidence-based reasoning and respectful acknowledgment of opposing viewpoints.

About this Text
Purpose for Reading
Students will explore the persuasive writing process and practice constructing arguments that are supported by credible evidence.

Vocabulary
Persuasive: using arguments or reasoning to convince someone
Thesis: the main claim or argument an essay makes
Evidence: facts, examples, or quotes that support a claim
Counterargument: an opposing point of view
Credible: trustworthy and reliable

LESSON OVERVIEW
This unit teaches students how to write persuasive essays on social issues they care about. Over the course of this lesson, students will:
1. Identify a social issue they want to address
2. Develop a clear thesis statement
3. Research and organize supporting evidence
4. Address potential counterarguments
5. Write, revise, and present their essay

WHOLE CLASS READING
Today we're going to start by looking at what makes an argument persuasive. Turn to your neighbor and talk about a time when someone convinced you to change your mind about something. What did they say? How did they convince you?

Now, let's look at a model persuasive essay. Pay attention to:
- The clear statement of what the writer believes
- The reasons and evidence they provide
- How they handle disagreement

PARTNER READING
Work with a partner to analyze the essay. Answer these questions together:
1. What is the writer trying to convince us of?
2. What evidence do they use?
3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?

DURING READING QUESTIONS
A. Think & Share
After reading the introduction, what do you think this essay will argue?

B. Turn & Talk
With your neighbor, discuss: Do you agree or disagree with the writer's main point so far?

C. Write
Jot down one piece of evidence the writer uses and whether it supports their claim.

D. Find Evidence
Highlight one sentence where the writer acknowledges a different point of view.

Independent Practice
Now it's your turn. Choose a social issue you care about—it could be about your school, your community, or something global. Write down:
1. The issue: _______________
2. Your position (what do you believe): _______________
3. Three reasons why you believe this: _______________
4. Evidence for each reason: _______________

Student-Led Discussion
In small groups, share your chosen issues. Help each other by asking:
- Is your position clear?
- Are your reasons strong?
- What evidence could support this better?

Suggested Pacing
Minutes 0-5: Introduction and definition of persuasive writing
Minutes 5-20: Read and analyze the model essay together
Minutes 20-30: Partner reading and analysis questions
Minutes 30-40: Independent practice—students choose their issue
Minutes 40-45: Small group discussion and peer feedback

[1] A persuasive essay is a piece of writing where the author tries to convince the reader to believe something or to take action. Unlike informative writing, which just explains facts, persuasive writing takes a position and supports it with reasons and evidence.

[2] A strong persuasive essay has three main parts. First, it clearly states what the author believes (the thesis). Second, it provides solid reasons and evidence that support this belief. Third, it acknowledges that other people might disagree and explains why the author's position is still stronger.

[3] When you write persuasively, you need to think about who your audience is. If you're writing to convince other seventh graders, you might use examples from school. If you're writing to convince adults or government leaders, you might use statistics and news articles. Knowing your audience helps you choose the best evidence.

[4] One important technique in persuasive writing is called addressing counterarguments. This means you mention what people who disagree with you might say, and then explain why you still think you're right. For example, you might write: "Some people say that video games are harmless, but research shows that too much screen time affects sleep."

VOCABULARY
Persuasive — writing that tries to convince someone to believe or do something
Thesis — the main claim or argument of an essay
Evidence — facts, examples, or quotes that support a claim
Counterargument — an opposing point of view or argument
Credible — trustworthy and based on reliable sources
Claim — a statement that something is true

ABOUT THIS TEXT
Purpose for Reading
The purpose of this lesson is to understand how to write persuasive essays. Students will learn the parts of a persuasive essay, practice analyzing one, and begin developing their own persuasive argument on a social issue.

Notes to Teachers
This lesson introduces the persuasive writing process. Students may choose any social issue they care about—school policies, environmental concerns, community problems, or global issues. Encourage them to pick something they have genuine interest in, as this will make their writing more authentic and engaging.

Support students in finding credible evidence for their claims. You may want to have them research in the library or online to strengthen their arguments.

HOW DO I FACILITATE THIS LESSON?
1. Start with a conversation about times students have been persuaded. This activates prior knowledge.
2. Read the model essay aloud or have students read it silently, then discuss as a class.
3. Have students complete the partner reading activity to analyze the essay's structure.
4. Guide the independent practice section, helping students brainstorm and narrow their topic.
5. End with small group discussions where students can get feedback on their positions and evidence.

TEACHER COPY: ANSWER KEY
Partner Reading Questions
1. What is the writer trying to convince us of?
[Model answer: The writer is trying to convince us that our school should have a longer lunch period because students need more time to eat, socialize, and recharge.]

2. What evidence do they use?
[Model answer: They mention that students rush through lunch, that social time is important for mental health, and that teachers report students are less focused in afternoon classes when they haven't had adequate break time.]

3. Is their argument convincing? Why or why not?
[This will vary, but look for students to acknowledge the strengths of the evidence and whether they found it persuasive.]

Paragraphs 1-3: Introduction and Background
These paragraphs establish the issue and explain why it matters.

Paragraph 4: The Thesis
This is where the writer clearly states their position.

Paragraphs 5-7: Supporting Reasons
Each paragraph gives one reason and evidence for why the thesis is true.

Paragraph 8: Counterargument
The writer acknowledges an opposing view.

Paragraph 9: Conclusion
The writer restates their position and calls the reader to action or reflection.

MULTIPLE-CHOICE PRACTICE QUESTIONS

1. Which of the following best describes a thesis statement?
A. A question about the topic
B. A clear statement of the author's position or belief
C. A list of all the evidence in the essay
D. The conclusion of the essay
[Answer: B]

2. What is a counterargument?
A. A reason that supports the main claim
B. An opposing point of view that the author addresses
C. The evidence used in the essay
D. The introduction to the essay
[Answer: B]

3. If you were writing a persuasive essay to your school principal about extending lunch time, which evidence would be MOST credible?
A. "My friend thinks we need more time to eat"
B. A study showing that students with longer lunch breaks have better afternoon attendance
C. "Food tastes better when you eat it slowly"
D. "I saw someone rushing through lunch yesterday"
[Answer: B]

4. Which sentence is the best thesis statement for a persuasive essay?
A. "Many students struggle with homework."
B. "Our school should require students to have one free period for homework instead of study hall."
C. "Some people like homework, and some people don't."
D. "Homework is a topic that people discuss."
[Answer: B]
